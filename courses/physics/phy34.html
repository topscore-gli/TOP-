⚡ Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

JAMB Physics • motor rule • force on conductor • parallel conductors • moving charges • DC motor • galvanometer conversion

🎯 Learning Objective

This chapter explains force on current-carrying conductors, force between parallel wires, force on moving charges, DC motors, electromagnets, microphones, electrical instruments, and galvanometer conversions.

  • Force on a conductor is given by F = BIL sin θ
  • Parallel currents in the same direction attract
  • A moving charge in a magnetic field experiences F = qvB sin θ
  • Galvanometers can be converted into ammeters and voltmeters
🧠 Big idea

Magnetic fields can push currents and moving charges, and this effect is the basis of motors and many electrical measuring instruments.

Force on a current-carrying conductor

Force formula
F = BIL sin θ
  • Maximum force when θ = 90°
  • Zero force when θ = 0°
Current, field, and force directions
Field, current, and force are mutually perpendicular in the motor effect.
Calculation 1: Force on a Conductor

A 25 cm wire carries 4 A and is perpendicular to a magnetic field of 0.5 T. Find the force.

L = 0.25 m

F = BIL = 0.5 × 4 × 0.25 = 0.5 N

Answer: 0.5 N

2️⃣ Force Between Parallel Current-Carrying Conductors

Two parallel current-carrying wires exert magnetic forces on each other.

Force per unit length
F/L = (μ₀I₁I₂)/(2πd)
  • Same current direction → attractive force
  • Opposite current direction → repulsive force
Parallel conductors
Parallel currents in the same direction attract each other.

The definition of the ampere historically comes from the force between two parallel conductors.

Calculation 2: Force Between Parallel Conductors

Two long parallel wires 0.2 m apart carry currents of 5 A and 3 A in the same direction. Find the force per metre.

F/L = (4π × 10⁻⁷ × 5 × 3)/(2π × 0.2)

F/L = 1.5 × 10⁻⁵ N/m

Answer: 1.5 × 10⁻⁵ N/m, attractive

3️⃣ Force on a Moving Charge in a Magnetic Field

Lorentz force

Force on moving charge
F = qvB sin θ

For a positive charge, use the usual direction rule. For an electron, the force direction is opposite.

Circular motion

If a charged particle enters a field perpendicularly, the magnetic force acts as centripetal force.

Radius of path
r = mv/(qB)
Charged particle in magnetic field
A perpendicular magnetic force changes direction of motion, producing a circular path.
Calculation 3: Force on Moving Charge

An electron of charge 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C moves at 2 × 10⁶ m/s perpendicular to a field of 0.1 T. Find the force.

F = qvB = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 2 × 10⁶ × 0.1

F = 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁴ N

Answer: 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁴ N
Calculation 4: Radius of Circular Path

A proton of mass 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg and charge 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C enters a 0.2 T field at 10⁵ m/s. Find the radius.

r = mv/(qB)

r = (1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ × 10⁵)/(1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 0.2) = 5.22 × 10⁻³ m

Answer: 5.22 mm

4️⃣ The DC Motor

A DC motor works because a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field experiences a turning effect.

Main parts

  • Armature or coil
  • Field magnets
  • Commutator or split ring
  • Brushes
Torque on a coil
τ = BANI sin θ
Basic DC motor idea
The split-ring commutator reverses current every half turn to keep the coil rotating.

5️⃣ Electromagnets

An electromagnet is produced when current flows through a solenoid wound around a soft iron core.

Factors affecting strength

  • Number of turns in the coil
  • Magnitude of current
  • Presence of a soft iron core
  • Shape of the pole pieces

Applications

  • Electric bells
  • Relays
  • Magnetic cranes
  • MRI machines
  • Loudspeakers

6️⃣ Carbon Microphone

A carbon microphone works by variable resistance.

  • Sound waves make the diaphragm vibrate
  • The diaphragm presses carbon granules
  • The resistance changes
  • The current changes accordingly

It needs an external power source, unlike some other microphone types.

7️⃣ Moving-Coil and Moving-Iron Instruments

Moving-coil instruments

  • Work with a coil in a permanent magnetic field
  • Measure DC only unless a rectifier is used
  • Have a linear scale
  • Are sensitive and accurate

Moving-iron instruments

  • Work by magnetizing soft iron pieces
  • Measure both AC and DC
  • Have a non-linear scale
  • Are less sensitive but more rugged

8️⃣ Converting Galvanometers

Galvanometer basics

  • Detects very small currents
  • Full-scale current is I₉
  • Internal resistance is R₉

Conversion to ammeter

Ammeter conversion uses a low-resistance shunt in parallel.

Shunt resistance
Rₛ = (I₉R₉)/(I - I₉)

Conversion to voltmeter

Voltmeter conversion uses a high resistance in series.

Multiplier resistance
Rₘ = V/I₉ - R₉
Galvanometer conversions
Ammeter uses a shunt in parallel; voltmeter uses a multiplier in series.
Calculation 5: Galvanometer to Ammeter

A galvanometer has R₉ = 50 Ω and full-scale current I₉ = 1 mA. Find the shunt for a 5 A ammeter.

Rₛ = (0.001 × 50)/(5 - 0.001)

Rₛ ≈ 0.01 Ω

Answer: 0.01 Ω
Calculation 6: Galvanometer to Voltmeter

The same galvanometer is to read 10 V full scale. Find the multiplier resistance.

Rₘ = 10/0.001 - 50 = 9950 Ω

Answer: 9950 Ω

9️⃣ Galvanometer Sensitivity

Current sensitivity

Current sensitivity
θ/I = BAN/K

Voltage sensitivity

Voltage sensitivity
θ/V = BAN/(KR₉)

Factors affecting sensitivity

  • Magnetic field strength
  • Number of turns
  • Area of coil
  • Torsional constant
  • Galvanometer resistance for voltage sensitivity

1️⃣1️⃣ Quick Revision Checklist

Must-know points

  • Apply Fleming’s left-hand rule correctly
  • Know when force is maximum and when it is zero
  • Same-direction parallel currents attract
  • Know the DC motor principle and torque formula
  • Ammeter uses parallel shunt; voltmeter uses series multiplier
  • Moving-coil is for DC; moving-iron is for AC and DC
💡 Exam tips

Always convert units carefully, watch the angle in sine terms, remember μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷, and use conventional current direction in magnetic force questions.

1️⃣2️⃣ Practice Questions for JAMB

The force on a current-carrying conductor is maximum when the conductor is:

(a) Parallel to the field
(b) Perpendicular to the field
(c) At 45° to the field
(d) Stationary

Answer: (b) Perpendicular to the field
Two parallel wires carrying currents in the same direction will:

(a) Repel
(b) Attract
(c) Have no force
(d) Rotate

Answer: (b) Attract
To convert a galvanometer to a voltmeter, you connect a:

(a) Low resistance in parallel
(b) High resistance in series
(c) Low resistance in series
(d) Battery in parallel

Answer: (b) High resistance in series
