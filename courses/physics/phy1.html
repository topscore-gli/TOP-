1️⃣ Length, Area and Volume

Length is the distance between two points. Its SI unit is the metre (m).

Area is the size of a surface. Its SI unit is m².

Volume is the amount of space occupied. Its SI unit is m³.

Basic formulas
Area of rectangle = length × breadth
Volume of cuboid = length × breadth × height
Volume of cylinder = πr²h
Surface area of sphere = 4πr²
Length, area and volume idea
Length is 1-dimensional, area is 2-dimensional, and volume is 3-dimensional.

3️⃣ Vernier Calipers

Vernier calipers are used for measuring external diameter, internal diameter, and depth.

How to read vernier calipers
Reading = Main Scale Reading + (Vernier coincidence × Least Count)
Least Count = 0.01 cm
Simple vernier calipers reading idea
First read the main scale, then add the coinciding vernier division multiplied by the least count.

Zero error correction: Positive error is subtracted. Negative error is added.

🧠 Memory aid

PESA = Positive Error Subtract, Negative Error Add.

Example 1: Basic Reading

Main scale = 3.2 cm, 4th vernier division coincides.

Step 1: Vernier reading = 4 × 0.01 = 0.04 cm

Step 2: Total = 3.2 + 0.04 = 3.24 cm

Answer: 3.24 cm
Example 2: Zero Error Correction

Observed reading = 2.56 cm, zero error = +0.03 cm.

Step 1: Positive error is subtracted

Step 2: Correct reading = 2.56 - 0.03 = 2.53 cm

Answer: 2.53 cm
Example 3: Volume of a Cylinder

Length = 5.45 cm, diameter = 2.16 cm.

Step 1: Radius = 2.16 / 2 = 1.08 cm

Step 2: V = πr²h

Step 3: V = (22/7) × (1.08)² × 5.45 ≈ 19.98 cm³

Answer: 20.0 cm³

4️⃣ Micrometer Screw Gauge

A micrometer screw gauge measures very small lengths such as the diameter of a wire.

How to read micrometer
Reading = Sleeve Reading + (Thimble Reading × Least Count)
Least Count = 0.01 mm = 0.001 cm
Simple micrometer reading idea
Read the sleeve first, then add the thimble reading multiplied by the least count.
Example 4: Basic Reading

Sleeve = 5.5 mm, thimble = 28 divisions.

Step 1: Thimble reading = 28 × 0.01 = 0.28 mm

Step 2: Total = 5.5 + 0.28 = 5.78 mm

Answer: 5.78 mm = 0.578 cm
Example 5: Zero Error Correction

Observed reading = 3.25 mm, zero error = -0.04 mm.

Step 1: Negative error is added

Step 2: Correct reading = 3.25 + 0.04 = 3.29 mm

Answer: 3.29 mm
Example 6: Error Analysis

Readings: 2.14, 2.16, 2.15, 2.17, 2.13 mm

Step 1: Mean = (2.14+2.16+2.15+2.17+2.13)/5 = 2.15 mm

Step 2: Absolute errors = 0.01, 0.01, 0, 0.02, 0.02

Step 3: Mean absolute error = (0.01+0.01+0+0.02+0.02)/5 = 0.012 mm

Final result: 2.15 ± 0.01 mm

5️⃣ Metre Rule and Measuring Cylinder

Metre Rule

The metre rule measures ordinary lengths. Its least count is usually 0.1 cm.

Measuring Cylinder

A measuring cylinder measures liquid volume and irregular solid volume by displacement.

Volume by displacement
Volume of irregular solid = final volume - initial volume.
Example 7: Percentage Error in Volume

Dimensions of block = 8.5 cm × 3.2 cm × 1.5 cm, each with ±0.1 cm error.

Step 1: Volume = 8.5 × 3.2 × 1.5 = 40.8 cm³

Step 2: Relative error = (0.1/8.5) + (0.1/3.2) + (0.1/1.5)

Step 3: = 0.1098

Step 4: Percentage error = 0.1098 × 100 ≈ 11.0%

Answers: 40.8 cm³, 11.0%
Example 8: Volume by Displacement

Initial water level = 45 cm³, final level = 62 cm³.

Step 1: Volume of stone = 62 - 45

Answer: 17 cm³

6️⃣ Standard Form and Uncertainty

Standard form is used to write very large or very small numbers in a neat form.

Standard form pattern
a × 10ⁿ, where 1 ≤ a < 10
Example 10: Standard Form

456789 = 4.57 × 10⁵

0.00003456 = 3.46 × 10⁻⁵

299792458 = 3.00 × 10⁸

Example 11: Uncertainty Expression

Length = 25.4 cm with uncertainty ±0.2 cm

Step 1: Write as 25.4 ± 0.2 cm

Step 2: Percentage error = (0.2/25.4) × 100 ≈ 0.79%

Answer: 0.8%

7️⃣ Area and Volume Calculations

Example 12: Wire Volume

Length = 50 cm, diameter = 0.50 mm

Step 1: Radius = 0.25 mm = 0.025 cm

Step 2: V = πr²h

Step 3: V = (22/7) × (0.025)² × 50 ≈ 0.0982 cm³

Answer: 9.82 × 10⁻² cm³
Example 13: Surface Area of a Sphere

Radius = 3.5 cm

Step 1: A = 4πr²

Step 2: A = 4 × (22/7) × (3.5)² = 154 cm²

Answer: 154 cm²

🔬 Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis is used to check whether an equation is reasonable or dimensionally correct.

Rule: All terms added or equated in a physical equation must have the same dimensions.

Example 14: Check s = ut + ½at²

Step 1: [s] = [L]

Step 2: [ut] = [LT⁻¹][T] = [L]

Step 3: [at²] = [LT⁻²][T²] = [L]

Conclusion: Equation is dimensionally consistent ✓
Example 15: Verify T = 2π√(l/g)

Step 1: [l/g] = [L]/[LT⁻²] = [T²]

Step 2: √[T²] = [T]

Conclusion: Dimensions match ✓
Example 16: Find dimensions of a, b and c in x = a + bt + ct²

Step 1: Since x is distance, [x] = [L]

Step 2: [a] = [L]

Step 3: [b] = [L]/[T] = [LT⁻¹]

Step 4: [c] = [L]/[T²] = [LT⁻²]

↔️ Scalar and Vector Quantities

Type Meaning Examples
Scalar Has magnitude only Mass, time, distance, speed, energy, temperature, density
Vector Has magnitude and direction Displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum, electric field

Vector addition
R = √(P² + Q² + 2PQcosθ)
If θ = 90°, then R = √(P² + Q²)
Distance vs displacement
Distance is total path length, but displacement is the straight-line change in position with direction.
Example 17: Position and Displacement

A moves from A(2,3) to B(5,7)

Step 1: Displacement vector = (5-2, 7-3) = (3,4)

Step 2: Distance = √(3² + 4²) = 5 m

Step 3: Direction = tan⁻¹(4/3) = 53.1°

Answers: Displacement = (3,4) m, Distance = 5 m, Direction = 53.1°
