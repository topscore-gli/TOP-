💡 Light Energy

JAMB Physics • sources of light • luminous objects • shadows • eclipses • pin-hole camera • wave equation

🎯 Learning Objective

This chapter explains sources of light, luminous and non-luminous objects, the wave equation for light, formation of shadows and eclipses, and pin-hole camera magnification.

  • Light is an electromagnetic wave
  • Luminous objects produce their own light
  • Non-luminous objects reflect light
  • The basic wave formula is v = fλ
🧠 Big idea

We see most objects not because they make light, but because they reflect light from luminous sources.

Natural and artificial light sources

Luminous vs non-luminous objects

Luminous objects produce their own light, while non-luminous objects do not produce light but reflect light from other sources.

  • Luminous: Sun, stars, electric bulb, candle flame, firefly
  • Non-luminous: Moon, table, human body, planets
⚠️ JAMB trap

The Moon is non-luminous. It shines because it reflects sunlight.

2️⃣ Propagation of Light

Light travels as an electromagnetic wave.

Wave equation
v = fλ
  • v = speed of light
  • f = frequency
  • λ = wavelength

Key values to memorize

Medium Speed of Light
Vacuum / air3.0 × 10⁸ m/s
Water2.25 × 10⁸ m/s
Glass2.0 × 10⁸ m/s
← Swipe table to see more →
Light speed in different media
Light travels fastest in vacuum and more slowly in denser media like water and glass.
Calculation 1: Wave Equation

A light wave has frequency 6.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz. Find its wavelength in vacuum.

Step: λ = c/f = (3.0 × 10⁸)/(6.0 × 10¹⁴)

λ = 5.0 × 10⁻⁷ m = 500 nm

Answer: 500 nm

3️⃣ Formation of Shadows and Eclipses

Shadows form because light travels in straight lines and can be blocked by opaque objects.

Parts of a shadow

  • Umbra: region of total darkness
  • Penumbra: region of partial darkness
Umbra and penumbra
The umbra is the complete shadow, while the penumbra is the partial shadow.

4️⃣ The Pin-Hole Camera

A pin-hole camera works on the principle that light travels in straight lines. Rays from the object pass through a tiny hole and form an inverted image on the screen.

Magnification formula
hᵢ/hₒ = v/u = m
  • hᵢ = image height
  • hₒ = object height
  • v = image distance
  • u = object distance
  • m = magnification

Image characteristics

  • Real
  • Inverted
  • Laterally inverted
  • Size depends on distances
Pin-hole camera principle
The top of the object forms the lower part of the image, so the image is inverted.
Calculation 2: Image Height

An object 2 m tall is 4 m from a pin-hole camera. If the screen is 0.5 m from the pinhole, find the image height.

Step: hᵢ/hₒ = v/u

hᵢ/2 = 0.5/4

hᵢ = 0.25 m = 25 cm

Answer: 25 cm
Calculation 3: Finding Object Height

A pin-hole camera has a screen 10 cm from the pinhole. An object 5 m away produces an image 2 cm tall. Find the height of the object and the magnification.

Convert: 5 m = 500 cm

2/hₒ = 10/500

hₒ = 100 cm = 1 m

m = 2/100 = 0.02

Answer: 1 m and 0.02
Calculation 4: Finding Screen Distance

An object 6 m high is 12 m away. If the image is 10 cm high, how far is the screen from the pinhole?

Convert: 10 cm = 0.1 m

0.1/6 = v/12

v = 0.2 m = 20 cm

Answer: 20 cm

6️⃣ JAMB Exam Tips

  • Light travels fastest in vacuum at 3 × 10⁸ m/s
  • The Moon is non-luminous
  • Pin-hole camera images are inverted
  • Use consistent units before calculation
  • Solar eclipse and lunar eclipse are often tested as comparisons
🧠 Safety note

Never look directly at a solar eclipse. It can seriously damage the eyes.

7️⃣ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Calling the Moon a luminous object
  • Forgetting that pin-hole camera images are inverted
  • Mixing up solar and lunar eclipses
  • Using cm and m carelessly in calculations
  • Forgetting that light slows down in denser media like water and glass
