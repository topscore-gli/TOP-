🎯 Learning Objective

This chapter explains saturated and unsaturated vapours, saturated vapour pressure, boiling point, barometer method, humidity, dew point, and the formation of dew, mist, fog, and rain.

  • Unsaturated vapour can still take in more vapour

  • Boiling occurs when S.V.P equals external pressure
  • Humidity tells how much water vapour air contains
🧠 Big idea

Saturated vapour pressure depends mainly on temperature, and many weather effects happen when air cools to the dew point.

1️⃣ Types of Vapours

Unsaturated vapour

  • Not in contact with its own liquid
  • Can hold more vapour
  • Obeys gas laws
  • Pressure changes when compressed or cooled

Saturated vapour

  • In contact with its own liquid in a closed space
  • Evaporation rate equals condensation rate
  • Does not obey gas laws normally
  • Has the maximum vapour possible at that temperature

2️⃣ Saturated Vapour Pressure

Saturated vapour pressure is the pressure exerted by a vapour in dynamic equilibrium with its liquid in a closed container at a given temperature.

Boiling condition
Boiling occurs when S.V.P = external pressure
  • At sea level, water boils at 100°C because its S.V.P equals atmospheric pressure
  • Lower external pressure gives lower boiling point
  • Higher external pressure gives higher boiling point

Factors affecting S.V.P

  • Temperature: S.V.P increases as temperature increases
  • Nature of liquid: more volatile liquids have higher S.V.P
  • Not affected by: container volume, amount of liquid, presence of other gases

Pressure cookers work because higher pressure raises the boiling point, so food cooks faster.

3️⃣ Determination of S.V.P by Barometer Method

The barometer method uses a mercury column. When vapour is introduced, it pushes the mercury column downward.

Barometer method formula
S.V.P = h₁ - h₂
  • h₁ = height of mercury column without vapour
  • h₂ = height of mercury column after vapour is introduced
Barometer method idea
The fall in mercury column height gives the saturated vapour pressure.

4️⃣ Formation of Dew, Mist, Fog, and Rain

These are all caused by condensation of water vapour when air is cooled to or below its dew point.

5️⃣ Humidity and Dew Point

Absolute humidity

The mass of water vapour present in unit volume of air, usually in g/m³.

Relative humidity

Relative humidity is the ratio of actual water vapour present to the amount needed for saturation at the same temperature, expressed as a percentage.

Relative humidity formulas
R.H = (actual vapour / saturation vapour) × 100%
R.H = (S.V.P at dew point / S.V.P at air temperature) × 100%

Dew point

Dew point is the temperature at which air becomes saturated. At this point, relative humidity is 100% and condensation begins.

🧠 Key fact

If relative humidity is 100%, then the dew point is the same as the air temperature.

6️⃣ Hygrometry

A wet and dry bulb hygrometer is used to estimate humidity.

  • Dry bulb measures actual air temperature
  • Wet bulb is cooled by evaporation from wet cloth

How it works

  • Low humidity → more evaporation → bigger temperature difference
  • High humidity → less evaporation → smaller temperature difference
  • At 100% humidity → no evaporation → both readings are equal
Wet and dry bulb hygrometer
The wet bulb usually reads lower because evaporation cools it.

To use a hygrometric table, record dry bulb temperature, find the wet bulb depression, then read off the relative humidity.

7️⃣ Essential Calculations for JAMB

Calculation 1: S.V.P from Barometer Readings

Mercury stands at 760 mm when dry and 710 mm when water vapour is introduced. Find the S.V.P.

Step: S.V.P = h₁ - h₂ = 760 - 710 = 50 mmHg

Answer: 50 mmHg
Calculation 2: Relative Humidity from Density

Air at 25°C contains 9.2 g/m³ of water vapour. Saturation vapour density at 25°C is 23.0 g/m³. Find the relative humidity.

Step: R.H = (9.2/23.0) × 100 = 40%

Answer: 40%
Calculation 3: Finding Dew Point

The air temperature is 30°C and relative humidity is 60%. Saturation vapour density is 30.4 g/m³ at 30°C and 19.8 g/m³ at 20°C. Estimate the dew point.

Step 1: Actual vapour density = 0.60 × 30.4 = 18.24 g/m³

Step 2: This is close to the saturation value at 20°C

Answer: about 20°C
Calculation 4: Boiling Point at High Altitude

At what temperature will water boil where atmospheric pressure is 600 mmHg, if water has S.V.P of 600 mmHg at 94°C?

Step: Boiling occurs when S.V.P = external pressure

Answer: 94°C
Calculation 5: Relative Humidity Using S.V.P

S.V.P at 20°C is 17.5 mmHg and at 10°C is 9.2 mmHg. If the dew point is 10°C on a 20°C day, find the relative humidity.

Step: R.H = (9.2/17.5) × 100 = 52.6%

Answer: about 53%
Calculation 6: Pressure Cooker

A pressure cooker cooks at 120°C. If the S.V.P of water at 120°C is 1489 mmHg, find the pressure inside. Take atmospheric pressure as 760 mmHg.

Absolute pressure: 1489 mmHg

Gauge pressure: 1489 - 760 = 729 mmHg

Answer: 1489 mmHg absolute or 729 mmHg gauge
Calculation 7: Hygrometer Reading

A hygrometer reads 20°C on the dry bulb and 16°C on the wet bulb. Given that for dry bulb 20°C, a difference of 4°C corresponds to 66%, find the relative humidity.

Step 1: Wet bulb depression = 20 - 16 = 4°C

Step 2: Read from table

Answer: 66%

8️⃣ Quick Revision Notes

Fast memory points

  • Saturated vapour is in equilibrium with its liquid
  • S.V.P depends mainly on temperature
  • Boiling point is reached when S.V.P equals atmospheric pressure
  • Relative humidity = actual ÷ saturation × 100%
  • Dew point is the temperature where air becomes saturated
  • Evaporation can happen at all temperatures, but boiling happens at a fixed point

9️⃣ Common Exam Tricks

  • Saturated vapour does not obey Boyle’s law the way unsaturated vapour does
  • S.V.P increases with temperature, and the increase is not simply linear
  • Boiling point decreases at high altitude because pressure is lower
  • At 100% relative humidity, dew point equals air temperature
  • Adding salt raises boiling point
