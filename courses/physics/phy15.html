🌡️ Thermal Expansion

JAMB Physics • linear expansivity • volume expansivity • liquids in containers • anomalous expansion of water

🎯 Learning Objective

This chapter explains how solids and liquids expand when heated, how to calculate linear and volume expansivities, how to distinguish real and apparent expansion of liquids, and why water behaves abnormally between 0°C and 4°C.

  • Heating makes particles vibrate more and spread farther apart
  • Solids expand in length, area, and volume
  • Liquids show real and apparent expansion
  • Water behaves unusually between 0°C and 4°C
🧠 Big idea

Expansion happens in all directions, but the way we measure it depends on whether we are looking at length, area, or volume.

1️⃣ Foundation Concept

When matter is heated, its particles gain energy and vibrate more vigorously. This makes the substance expand.

Particles spread out when heated
Heating increases spacing between particles, so the body expands.

4️⃣ Expansion of Liquids

The container problem

When a liquid in a container is heated, both the liquid and the container expand. This is why we distinguish between real and apparent expansion.

Type Definition Formula Key Point
Real expansion (γᵣ) Actual expansion of the liquid γᵣ = γₐ + γᵍ True molecular expansion
Apparent expansion (γₐ) What is observed in the container γₐ = ΔV_apparent/(V₀ΔT) Usually smaller than real expansion
Container expansion (γᵍ) Expansion of the bottle or glass γᵍ = 3αᵍ Must be included
← Swipe table to see more →
Most important liquid formula
γᵣ = γₐ + γᵍ

Real expansion is always greater than apparent expansion because the container also expands.

Real vs apparent expansion
The liquid rises, but the container also expands. What you see is the apparent expansion.

🧠 Key fact

Water has maximum density at 4°C.

This is why lakes freeze from the top. Ice floats on the surface while denser 4°C water stays below, allowing aquatic life to survive.

7️⃣ Essential Calculations for JAMB

Example 1: Basic Linear Expansion

A copper rod is 50 cm long at 20°C and heated to 100°C. Find its new length. Take α = 17×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹.

Step 1: ΔT = 100 - 20 = 80°C

Step 2: L = L₀(1 + αΔT)

Step 3: L = 50(1 + 17×10⁻⁶ × 80)

Step 4: L = 50.068 cm

Answer: 50.068 cm
Example 2: Finding Original Length

A rod expands by 0.5 mm when heated from 20°C to 70°C. If α = 20×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹, find the original length.

Step 1: ΔL = L₀αΔT

Step 2: 0.0005 = L₀ × 20×10⁻⁶ × 50

Step 3: L₀ = 0.5 m

Answer: 0.5 m
Example 3: Volume Expansion of a Solid

An aluminium cube of side 10 cm at 0°C is heated to 100°C. Find the increase in volume. Take α = 23×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹.

Step 1: γ = 3α = 69×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

Step 2: V₀ = 10³ = 1000 cm³

Step 3: ΔV = V₀γΔT = 1000 × 69×10⁻⁶ × 100

Step 4: ΔV = 6.9 cm³

Answer: 6.9 cm³
Example 4: Relationship Between Expansivities

If α = 19×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹, find the superficial expansivity.

Step: β = 2α = 38×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

Answer: 38 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹
Example 5: Real and Apparent Expansion

A glass bottle contains 500 cm³ of mercury at 20°C. At 100°C the mercury appears to rise by 6.5 cm³. Find: (a) apparent expansivity (b) real expansivity. Take γ_glass = 25×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹.

(a) γₐ = 6.5/(500 × 80) = 1.625×10⁻⁴ K⁻¹ = 162.5×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

(b) γᵣ = γₐ + γᵍ = 162.5×10⁻⁶ + 25×10⁻⁶ = 187.5×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

Answer: 162.5 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹ and 187.5 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹
Example 6: Density Change with Temperature

The density of a liquid at 20°C is 800 kg/m³. Find its density at 120°C if γ = 2.0×10⁻⁴ K⁻¹.

Step 1: ρ = ρ₀/(1 + γΔT)

Step 2: ρ = 800/(1 + 2.0×10⁻⁴ × 100)

Step 3: ρ = 800/1.02 = 784.3 kg/m³

Answer: 784.3 kg/m³
Example 7: Bimetallic Strip

Brass has α = 19×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹ and iron has α = 12×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹. If both are 20 cm long at 20°C, find the difference in their expansions at 120°C.

Brass: ΔL = 20 × 19×10⁻⁶ × 100 = 0.038 cm

Iron: ΔL = 20 × 12×10⁻⁶ × 100 = 0.024 cm

Difference: 0.038 - 0.024 = 0.014 cm = 0.14 mm

Answer: 0.14 mm
Example 8: Anomalous Expansion of Water

Water has volume 1000 cm³ at 0°C. At what temperature is its volume minimum? Also find the volume at 2°C if γ = -4.0×10⁻⁵ K⁻¹.

Minimum volume: at 4°C

At 2°C: V = 1000(1 - 4.0×10⁻⁵ × 2)

V = 1000(0.99992) = 999.92 cm³

Answer: minimum at 4°C, volume = 999.92 cm³
Example 9: Expansion Gap

A steel rail is 20 m long at 10°C. Find the gap needed if the maximum temperature is 60°C. Take α = 11×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹.

Step: ΔL = L₀αΔT = 20 × 11×10⁻⁶ × 50 = 0.011 m = 11 mm

Answer: 11 mm
Example 10: Compound Liquid Expansion Problem

A glass flask of volume 1000 cm³ is filled with mercury at 0°C. At 100°C, 15.3 cm³ overflows. If the real expansivity of mercury is 182×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹, find: (a) apparent expansivity (b) real expansivity (c) linear expansivity of glass.

(a) γₐ = 15.3/(1000 × 100) = 153×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

(b) γᵣ = 182×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

(c) γᵍ = γᵣ - γₐ = 29×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

αᵍ = γᵍ/3 = 9.67×10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

Answer: 153 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹, 182 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹, 9.67 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

9️⃣ JAMB Exam Strategies

High-yield focus points

  • Real vs apparent expansion is tested often
  • Examples 5 and 10 are especially important
  • Be careful with units of length and volume
  • Remember that ΔT has same numerical value in K and °C
  • Do not confuse 2α with 3α
🧠 Most important distinction

Real expansion is what actually happens to the liquid. Apparent expansion is what you observe in the container.
