🧠 Big idea

Electrostatics is the study of charges at rest and the forces, fields, and potentials they create.

1️⃣ Fundamental Concepts of Charges

Positive and negative charges

All matter contains electric charges. The two basic types are:

  • Positive charge: carried by protons
  • Negative charge: carried by electrons

Key rule: Like charges repel, unlike charges attract.

2️⃣ Methods of Charging a Body

A. Charging by friction

When two different materials are rubbed together, electrons move from one to the other.

  • Glass rod + silk → glass becomes positive
  • Ebonite rod + fur → ebonite becomes negative
🧠 Memory tip

Glass is Posh (Positive), Ebonite is Negative.

B. Charging by contact

A charged body touches a neutral body and transfers charge directly. Both end up with the same type of charge.

C. Charging by induction

A body is charged without contact by bringing a charged object near it, grounding, then removing the ground and the inducing object.

Induction produces a charge opposite to the inducing object.

Charging by induction
Charges separate inside the conductor, and grounding allows one type to escape.

3️⃣ The Electroscope

An electroscope is an instrument used to detect and test electric charges.

Main parts

  • Metal cap
  • Metal rod
  • Gold leaf
  • Insulating support
  • Earthed metal case

Uses

  • Detecting charge
  • Identifying charge type
  • Comparing magnitudes of charge
  • Testing conductors and insulators
Gold-leaf electroscope
When charge reaches the leaf, like charges repel and the leaf diverges.

4️⃣ Coulomb’s Inverse Square Law

The force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

Coulomb’s law
F = kq₁q₂/r²

Where:

  • F = force in newtons
  • q₁, q₂ = charges in coulombs
  • r = distance in metres
  • k = 9 × 10⁹ Nm²C⁻²

If distance doubles, the force becomes one-quarter.

Calculation 1: Coulomb’s Law

Two charges of +3 μC and −5 μC are 0.3 m apart. Find the force between them.

F = (9 × 10⁹)(3 × 10⁻⁶)(5 × 10⁻⁶)/(0.3)²

F = 1.5 N

Answer: 1.5 N, attractive
Calculation 2: Finding Distance

Two identical charges of +2 μC repel with a force of 0.36 N. Find their separation.

r² = kq²/F

r² = (9 × 10⁹)(2 × 10⁻⁶)²/0.36 = 0.1

r = √0.1 = 0.316 m

Answer: 0.316 m or 31.6 cm

5️⃣ Electric Field

An electric field is a region in which a charge experiences a force.

Electric field intensity
E = F/q = kQ/r²

Unit: N/C or V/m

Field patterns

  • Positive charge: lines point outward
  • Negative charge: lines point inward
  • Opposite charges: lines go from + to −
  • Parallel plates: nearly uniform field
Electric field patterns
Field lines leave positive charges and enter negative charges.
Calculation 3: Electric Field Intensity

Find the electric field intensity at 0.5 m from a point charge of +4 μC.

E = (9 × 10⁹)(4 × 10⁻⁶)/(0.5)²

E = 1.44 × 10⁵ N/C

Answer: 1.44 × 10⁵ N/C

6️⃣ Electric Potential

Electric potential is the work done per unit positive charge in bringing a test charge from infinity to a point.

Electric potential
V = kQ/r
Potential difference
ΔV = W/q
Field and potential in a uniform field
E = V/d

Unit of potential: volt (V)

Calculation 4: Electric Potential

Find the electric potential at 0.2 m from a charge of −6 μC.

V = (9 × 10⁹)(−6 × 10⁻⁶)/0.2

V = −2.7 × 10⁵ V

Answer: −2.7 × 10⁵ V
Calculation 5: Work Done

How much work is needed to move a charge of +5 μC through a potential difference of 200 V?

W = qΔV = (5 × 10⁻⁶)(200)

W = 1 × 10⁻³ J

Answer: 1 mJ
Calculation 6: Uniform Electric Field

Two plates are 2 cm apart and the p.d. between them is 400 V. Find the electric field and the force on an electron.

E = V/d = 400/0.02 = 2 × 10⁴ V/m

F = qE = (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹)(2 × 10⁴) = 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁵ N

Answer: 2 × 10⁴ V/m and 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁵ N
Calculation 7: Position of Zero Net Force

Two charges +4 μC and +6 μC are 0.5 m apart. Where should a −2 μC charge be placed between them for zero net force?

Let distance from +4 μC be x.

4/x² = 6/(0.5 − x)²

x = 0.225 m

Answer: 0.225 m or 22.5 cm from the +4 μC charge

7️⃣ Electric Discharge and Lightning

Electric discharge

Electric discharge is the sudden flow of charge when the potential difference becomes large enough to ionize the surrounding medium.

Lightning formation

  • Charges separate in clouds due to friction and updrafts
  • The base of the cloud becomes negatively charged
  • Positive charge is induced on the ground
  • When the electric field is strong enough, air breaks down and a spark occurs
Lightning and induced ground charge
Negative charge at the cloud base induces positive charge on the ground below.

Lightning conductor

A lightning conductor is a sharp metal rod fixed at the top of a building and connected to earth by a thick copper strip.

It protects buildings by providing a safe low-resistance path for charge to flow to the ground.

9️⃣ JAMB Exam Tips

High-score reminders

  • Always convert μC to C by multiplying by 10⁻⁶
  • Coulomb’s law uses r², not just r
  • Field lines go out of positive and into negative
  • Contact gives same charge, induction gives opposite charge
  • Potential is scalar, field is vector
⚠️ Common mistakes

Do not mix up charging by contact and induction, and do not forget that the test charge for field direction is taken as positive.
