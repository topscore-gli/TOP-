📋 Introduction to Elasticity

Elasticity is the property of a material that allows it to return to its original shape and size after a deforming force is removed.

Examples of elastic materials include springs, rubber bands, and metal wires — as long as they are not stretched beyond their limit.

  • If the material returns to its original shape, the deformation is elastic
  • If it does not return fully, the deformation is plastic
  • If too much force is applied, the material may break
🧠 Easy idea

Elasticity means “stretch and come back.” If it stretches and does not come back, elasticity has been exceeded.

1️⃣ Hooke’s Law

Hooke’s law states that, provided the elastic limit is not exceeded, the extension of an elastic material is directly proportional to the applied force.

Hooke’s law formula
F = ke
F = applied force (N)
e = extension (m)
k = spring constant or force constant (N/m)

A high value of k means the spring is stiff and difficult to stretch.

A low value of k means the spring stretches more easily.

Important: Hooke’s law only works up to the elastic limit.

Hooke’s law graph
The straight-line part shows Hooke’s law. After the elastic limit, the graph curves and the law no longer holds exactly.

2️⃣ Young’s Modulus

Young’s modulus measures the stiffness of a material. It shows how difficult it is to stretch a material.

Stress

Stress formula
Stress = F / A
F = force
A = cross-sectional area

Strain

Strain formula
Strain = e / L
e = extension
L = original length

Young’s modulus

Young’s modulus formula
E = Stress / Strain
E = (F/A) / (e/L)
E = FL / Ae

Unit of Young’s modulus is N/m² or Pa (pascal).

🧠 Meaning of Young’s modulus

The larger the Young’s modulus, the stiffer the material. Steel has a high value; rubber has a low value.

Stress-strain graph
P = proportional limit, E = elastic limit, Y = yield point, B = breaking point.

3️⃣ Force-Extension Curves

A force-extension graph shows how a material responds when more force is applied.

  • Proportional limit: graph is straight; Hooke’s law holds exactly
  • Elastic limit: material still returns to original length when force is removed
  • Yield point: permanent deformation begins
  • Breaking point: material snaps

A steep graph means the material is stiff. A long plastic region means the material can stretch a lot before breaking.

What JAMB may ask

  • Identify proportional limit
  • Identify elastic limit
  • State what yield point means
  • Interpret the area under the graph

4️⃣ Spring Balance as a Device for Measuring Force

A spring balance uses Hooke’s law to measure force.

When an object is hung on it, the spring extends. The extension is proportional to the force, so the balance can be calibrated in newtons.

For a hanging mass
Weight = mg
mg = ke
Spring balance
The greater the weight of the load, the more the spring stretches, and the higher the force reading.
  • Spring balance is accurate only within the elastic limit
  • If overloaded, it may give wrong readings permanently

5️⃣ Combination of Springs

Springs in series

When springs are joined end-to-end, the effective spring constant becomes smaller.

Series formula
1/keff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂
keff = k₁k₂ / (k₁ + k₂)

Springs in parallel

When springs are side-by-side, the effective spring constant becomes larger.

Parallel formula
keff = k₁ + k₂
🧠 Easy memory rule

Series makes the system softer. Parallel makes the system stiffer.

6️⃣ Work Done in Stretching a Spring

When you stretch a spring, you do work on it. That work is stored as elastic potential energy.

Work done in a spring
W = ½Fe
Since F = ke, then:
W = ½ke²
Area under force-extension graph
The work done is the area under the force-extension graph. For a spring obeying Hooke’s law, this area is a triangle.

7️⃣ Work Done Per Unit Volume

For a stretched wire or string, the work done per unit volume is:

Energy per unit volume
W / Volume = ½ × stress × strain

This tells us how much elastic energy is stored in each cubic metre of the material.

9️⃣ 10 JAMB-Style Examples with Step-by-Step Solutions

1. A spring extends by 0.05 m when a force of 10 N is applied. What is the spring constant?

A. 50 N/m B. 100 N/m C. 200 N/m D. 500 N/m

Step 1: Use Hooke’s law, F = ke

Step 2: k = F/e = 10/0.05

Step 3: k = 200 N/m

Answer: C. 200 N/m
2. A wire of length 3 m and cross-sectional area 2 × 10⁻⁶ m² is extended by 0.003 m under a force of 50 N. Calculate Young’s modulus.

A. 2.5 × 10¹⁰ Pa B. 5 × 10¹⁰ Pa C. 2.5 × 10⁹ Pa D. 5 × 10⁹ Pa

Step 1: Use E = FL/Ae

Step 2: E = (50 × 3) / (2 × 10⁻⁶ × 0.003)

Step 3: E = 150 / (6 × 10⁻⁹)

Step 4: E = 2.5 × 10¹⁰ Pa

Answer: A. 2.5 × 10¹⁰ Pa
3. In a force-extension graph, the straight-line portion ends at 20 N. What is the elastic limit if this point is the proportional limit?

A. 20 N B. Slightly above 20 N C. 0.02 m D. Cannot determine

Step 1: Proportional limit is where Hooke’s law holds exactly.

Step 2: Elastic limit is usually slightly beyond it.

Answer: B. Slightly above 20 N
4. A spring balance reads 5 N when a mass of 0.5 kg is hung on it. What is the extension if k = 100 N/m? (g = 10 m/s²)

A. 0.05 m B. 0.1 m C. 0.5 m D. 5 m

Step 1: Force = 5 N

Step 2: Use e = F/k = 5/100

Step 3: e = 0.05 m

Answer: A. 0.05 m
5. Work done in stretching a spring by 0.1 m is 2 J. What is the spring constant?

A. 200 N/m B. 400 N/m C. 40 N/m D. 20 N/m

Step 1: Use W = ½ke²

Step 2: 2 = ½ × k × (0.1)²

Step 3: 2 = 0.005k

Step 4: k = 400 N/m

Answer: B. 400 N/m
6. A material breaks at a stress of 10⁸ Pa. If its Young’s modulus is 2 × 10¹⁰ Pa, what is the strain at breaking point?

A. 0.005 B. 0.05 C. 0.5 D. 5

Step 1: Use E = stress/strain

Step 2: strain = stress/E

Step 3: strain = 10⁸ / (2 × 10¹⁰) = 0.005

Answer: A. 0.005
7. Two springs of constants 100 N/m and 200 N/m are connected in series. What is the effective spring constant?

A. 66.7 N/m B. 150 N/m C. 300 N/m D. 50 N/m

Step 1: For series, k = k₁k₂/(k₁+k₂)

Step 2: k = (100 × 200)/(100 + 200)

Step 3: k = 20000/300 = 66.7 N/m

Answer: A. 66.7 N/m
8. The work done per unit volume in an elastic string stretched to strain 0.02 with stress 4 × 10⁶ Pa is

A. 4 × 10⁴ J/m³ B. 8 × 10⁴ J/m³ C. 4 × 10⁶ J/m³ D. 2 × 10⁴ J/m³

Step 1: Use ½ × stress × strain

Step 2: ½ × 4 × 10⁶ × 0.02

Step 3: = 4 × 10⁴ J/m³

Answer: A. 4 × 10⁴ J/m³
9. A yield point on a force-extension curve indicates

A. Start of permanent deformation B. Breaking point C. Elastic limit D. Proportional limit

Step 1: Yield point means the material starts to deform permanently.

Answer: A. Start of permanent deformation
10. A catapult stretches a rubber string by 0.08 m with force rising to 50 N. What is the work done?

A. 2 J B. 4 J C. 8 J D. 16 J

Step 1: Work done in a spring-like elastic material is W = ½Fe

Step 2: W = ½ × 50 × 0.08

Step 3: W = 2 J

Answer: A. 2 J
