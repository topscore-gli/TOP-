🌡️ Temperature and Its Measurement

JAMB Physics • thermometric properties • temperature scales • thermometer types • calibration • conversions

🎯 Learning Objective

This chapter explains what temperature means, how it is measured, the physical properties used in thermometers, how thermometers are calibrated, and how to convert between Celsius, Kelvin, and Fahrenheit.

  • Temperature tells degree of hotness or coldness
  • Kelvin is the SI unit
  • Thermometric properties must change with temperature
  • Calibration often uses ice point and steam point
🧠 Big idea

Temperature is not heat. Temperature shows how hot something is, while heat is energy transferred because of temperature difference.

1️⃣ Concept of Temperature

Temperature is the degree of hotness or coldness of a body. It is related to the average kinetic energy of the molecules of a substance.

  • Heat flows from higher temperature to lower temperature
  • Temperature determines the direction of heat flow
  • SI unit = Kelvin (K)
  • Common practical unit = degree Celsius (°C)
Heat flows from hot to cold
Heat naturally flows from a region at higher temperature to a region at lower temperature.

3️⃣ Temperature Scales

Celsius scale

  • 0°C = ice point
  • 100°C = steam point
  • Interval divided into 100 equal parts

Kelvin scale

  • 0 K = absolute zero
  • 273.15 K = ice point
  • 373.15 K = steam point
  • No negative values in Kelvin scale
Main conversion formulas
TK = TC + 273
TC = TK - 273
TF = (9/5)TC + 32
Master scale relation
TC/100 = (TK - 273)/100 = (TF - 32)/180
Celsius and Kelvin scales
The Kelvin and Celsius scales have equal size divisions, but the Kelvin scale starts at absolute zero.

5️⃣ Calibration of Thermometers

Calibration means marking the scale of a thermometer using fixed reference points.

Steps in calibration

  1. Place the thermometer in melting ice and mark the level as 0°C
  2. Place it in steam above boiling water and mark the level as 100°C
  3. Divide the distance between the two marks into 100 equal parts
Linear calibration formula
(T - Tlower) / (Tupper - Tlower) = (X - Xlower) / (Xupper - Xlower)
Common JAMB form
T/100 = (X - X₀)/(X₁₀₀ - X₀)
Thermometer calibration
The lower fixed point is the ice point, and the upper fixed point is the steam point on the Celsius scale.

6️⃣ Essential JAMB Calculations

Calculation 1: Celsius to Kelvin

Convert 27°C to Kelvin.

Step: TK = TC + 273 = 27 + 273 = 300 K

Answer: 300 K
Calculation 2: Kelvin to Celsius

Convert 1273 K to Celsius.

Step: TC = 1273 - 273 = 1000°C

Answer: 1000°C
Calculation 3: Celsius to Fahrenheit

Convert 37°C to Fahrenheit.

Step: TF = (9/5)(37) + 32 = 66.6 + 32 = 98.6°F

Answer: 98.6°F
Calculation 4: Length Thermometer Calibration

A mercury thread is 4.0 cm at 0°C and 24.0 cm at 100°C. Find the temperature when the length is 14.0 cm.

Step 1: T/100 = (14.0 - 4.0)/(24.0 - 4.0)

Step 2: T/100 = 10/20 = 0.5

Step 3: T = 50°C

Answer: 50°C
Calculation 5: Resistance Thermometer

A platinum thermometer has resistance 5.0 Ω at ice point and 7.0 Ω at steam point. Find the temperature when resistance is 6.4 Ω.

Step 1: T/100 = (6.4 - 5.0)/(7.0 - 5.0)

Step 2: T/100 = 1.4/2 = 0.7

Step 3: T = 70°C

Answer: 70°C
Calculation 6: Gas Thermometer

A constant-volume gas thermometer reads 80 cmHg at 0°C and 110 cmHg at 100°C. Find the temperature when it reads 95 cmHg.

Step 1: T/100 = (95 - 80)/(110 - 80)

Step 2: T/100 = 15/30 = 0.5

Step 3: T = 50°C

In Kelvin: 50 + 273 = 323 K

Answer: 50°C or 323 K
Calculation 7: Thermocouple e.m.f.

A thermocouple produces 0.0 mV at 0°C and 5.0 mV at 100°C. Find the temperature at 3.5 mV.

Step 1: T/100 = (3.5 - 0)/(5.0 - 0)

Step 2: T/100 = 3.5/5 = 0.7

Step 3: T = 70°C

Answer: 70°C

8️⃣ Quick Revision Tips for JAMB

What to remember fast

  • Always add or subtract 273 for Kelvin and Celsius
  • Most calibration questions assume a linear relationship
  • Absolute zero is 0 K or about -273°C
  • Gas thermometers are the standard and most accurate
  • Use the correct calibration formula carefully

9️⃣ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using 273.15 instead of 273 in normal JAMB calculations when not required
  • Confusing heat with temperature
  • Thinking Kelvin can be negative
  • Using the wrong calibration formula
  • Forgetting that -40°C = 233 K, not a negative Kelvin value
