1️⃣ Models of the Atom and Their Limitations

Thomson’s plum pudding model

The atom was pictured as a sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded in it.

Limitation: It could not explain Rutherford’s alpha-particle scattering experiment.

Rutherford’s nuclear model

The atom has a tiny dense positive nucleus with electrons moving around it.

  • Could not explain line spectra
  • Predicted unstable atoms
  • Could not explain why orbiting electrons do not lose energy continuously

Bohr’s model

  • Electrons move in fixed stationary orbits
  • Each orbit has a definite energy
  • Energy is emitted or absorbed when electrons jump between levels
  • mvr = nh/2π
⭐ JAMB focus

Bohr’s model is the most testable one in this chapter, especially for hydrogen energy levels and spectra.

Simple Bohr atom idea
Bohr suggested that electrons occupy specific allowed energy levels.

3️⃣ Energy Levels and Spectra

In hydrogen, the electron can occupy only certain energy levels.

Energy of nth level
Eₙ = -13.6/n² eV
Photon energy
hf = E₂ - E₁
Rydberg formula
1/λ = Rₕ(1/n₁² - 1/n₂²)
Hydrogen energy levels
The electron emits a photon when it falls from a higher energy level to a lower one.
Calculation 1: Energy Levels

Find the energy of the photon emitted when an electron moves from n = 3 to n = 2 in hydrogen.

E₃ = -13.6/9 = -1.51 eV

E₂ = -13.6/4 = -3.40 eV

ΔE = E₃ - E₂ = 1.89 eV

Answer: 1.89 eV

4️⃣ Thermionic and Photoelectric Emissions

Thermionic emission

This is the emission of electrons from a heated metal surface.

Applications include cathode ray tubes, X-ray tubes, and electron guns.

Photoelectric effect

Electrons are emitted from a metal surface when light of sufficiently high frequency falls on it.

  • No emission below threshold frequency
  • Maximum kinetic energy depends on frequency
  • Number of electrons depends on light intensity

5️⃣ Einstein’s Equation and Stopping Potential

Einstein’s photoelectric equation
hf = W₀ + ½mv²max
Stopping potential
eVₛ = hf - W₀
Vₛ = h(f - f₀)/e

Work function W₀ is the minimum energy needed to remove an electron from the metal surface.

Calculation 2: Photoelectric Effect

Light of wavelength 400 nm falls on a metal of work function 2.0 eV. Find the photon energy, maximum kinetic energy, and threshold frequency.

E = hc/λ = 3.11 eV

Ek = 3.11 - 2.0 = 1.11 eV

f₀ = W₀/h = 4.83 × 10¹⁴ Hz

Answers: 3.11 eV, 1.11 eV, 4.83 × 10¹⁴ Hz
Calculation 3: Stopping Potential

For the same metal, find the stopping potential.

Vₛ = 1.11 V

Answer: 1.11 V

7️⃣ Production of X-Rays

In a Coolidge tube, electrons from a heated cathode are accelerated towards a metal target by high voltage.

Types of X-rays

  • Characteristic X-rays: produced when inner-shell electrons are removed and replaced
  • Bremsstrahlung: produced when fast electrons are suddenly decelerated

Properties of X-rays

  • High penetrating power
  • Travel in straight lines
  • Not deflected by electric or magnetic fields
  • Cause fluorescence
  • Ionize gases

A radioactive isotope has half-life 10 days. If the initial mass is 80 g, how much remains after 30 days?

n = 30/10 = 3

9️⃣ Half-Life and Decay Constant

Decay formulas
N = N₀e^(-λt)
N = N₀(1/2)^(t/T½)
A = λN
T½ = 0.693/λ
Calculation 5: Decay Constant

Find the decay constant of a substance with half-life 5 years.

λ = 0.693/5 = 0.139 year⁻¹

λ = 4.39 × 10⁻⁹ s⁻¹

Answer: 0.139 year⁻¹ or 4.39 × 10⁻⁹ s⁻¹
Calculation 6: Activity

A sample contains 10²⁰ nuclei and has decay constant 2 × 10⁻⁶ s⁻¹. Find the activity.

A = λN = 2 × 10⁻⁶ × 10²⁰ = 2 × 10¹⁴ Bq

Answer: 2 × 10¹⁴ Bq

🔟 Nuclear Energy: Fission and Fusion

Nuclear fission

Fission is the splitting of a heavy nucleus into lighter nuclei with energy release.

²³⁵₉₂U + ¹₀n → ¹⁴¹₅₆Ba + ⁹²₃₆Kr + 3¹₀n + Energy

Nuclear fusion

Fusion is the combination of light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus with energy release.

²₁H + ³₁H → ⁴₂He + ¹₀n + Energy

Fission powers nuclear reactors. Fusion powers the Sun.

Calculation 7: Energy from Fission

If the mass defect is 0.2 amu, calculate the energy released.

E = 0.2 × 931 = 186.2 MeV

= 2.98 × 10⁻¹¹ J

Answer: 186.2 MeV or 2.98 × 10⁻¹¹ J

1️⃣1️⃣ Binding Energy and Mass Defect

Mass defect
Δm = [Zmₚ + Nmₙ] - mₙucleus
Binding energy
ΔE = Δmc²
1 amu = 931 MeV

A nucleus with higher binding energy per nucleon is generally more stable.

Calculation 8: Binding Energy

Calculate binding energy per nucleon for helium-4 given mₚ = 1.0073 amu, mₙ = 1.0087 amu, and nuclear mass 4.0015 amu.

Mass of separate nucleons = 2(1.0073) + 2(1.0087) = 4.0320 amu

Δm = 4.0320 - 4.0015 = 0.0305 amu

Binding energy = 0.0305 × 931 = 28.4 MeV

Per nucleon = 28.4/4 = 7.1 MeV/nucleon

Answer: 7.1 MeV/nucleon

1️⃣2️⃣ Wave-Particle Duality

De Broglie proposed that matter has wave properties as well as particle properties.

De Broglie wavelength
λ = h/p = h/(mv)

Electron diffraction confirms the wave nature of electrons.

Calculation 9: De Broglie Wavelength

Find the de Broglie wavelength of an electron moving at 2 × 10⁶ m/s.

λ = h/(mv)

λ = 3.64 × 10⁻¹⁰ m = 0.364 nm

Answer: 3.64 × 10⁻¹⁰ m

1️⃣3️⃣ Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

It is impossible to know the exact position and exact momentum of a particle at the same time.

Uncertainty relation
Δx · Δp ≥ h/(4π)
Calculation 10: Uncertainty

An electron has speed 5 × 10⁵ m/s with uncertainty 1%. Find the minimum uncertainty in position.

Δv = 0.01 × 5 × 10⁵ = 5 × 10³ m/s

Δp = mΔv = 4.56 × 10⁻²⁷ kg·m/s

Δx ≥ 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ /(4π × 4.56 × 10⁻²⁷)

Δx ≥ 1.16 × 10⁻⁸ m

Answer: 1.16 × 10⁻⁸ m
