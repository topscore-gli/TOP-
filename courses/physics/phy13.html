🔥 Quantity of Heat

JAMB Physics • heat capacity • specific heat capacity • mixtures • electrical heating • heat calculations

🎯 Learning Objective

This chapter explains heat as a form of energy, the meaning of heat capacity and specific heat capacity, how they are determined, and how to solve the common numerical questions JAMB sets from this topic.

  • Heat is energy transferred because of temperature difference
  • Heat capacity refers to a whole body
  • Specific heat capacity refers to unit mass of a material
  • The main formula is Q = mcΔT
🧠 Big idea

If you know the mass, the material, and the temperature change, you can usually find the heat involved.

1️⃣ Heat as a Form of Energy

Heat is energy transferred from one body to another because of a temperature difference.

  • Heat flows from a hotter body to a colder body
  • Heat is measured in joules (J)
  • Another older unit is the calorie (cal)
Calorie conversion
1 calorie ≈ 4.2 J
Heat transfer
Heat moves from a hotter body to a colder body until thermal equilibrium is reached.

2️⃣ Heat Capacity

Heat capacity is the quantity of heat required to raise the temperature of a whole body by 1 kelvin or 1 degree Celsius.

Heat capacity formula
C = Q/ΔT
  • C = heat capacity
  • Q = quantity of heat supplied
  • ΔT = temperature change
  • Unit = J/K or J/°C

Heat capacity depends on the whole object, so it depends on both the material and the mass of the object.

3️⃣ Specific Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity is the quantity of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kilogram of a substance by 1 kelvin.

Master formulas
c = Q/(mΔT)
Q = mcΔT
  • c = specific heat capacity
  • m = mass
  • Q = heat energy
  • ΔT = temperature change

The unit of specific heat capacity is J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹.

🧠 Quick memory

If the question says “whole body,” think C. If it says “per kilogram,” think c.

6️⃣ Essential JAMB Calculations

Example 1: Basic Specific Heat Capacity

Calculate the temperature rise when 500 J of heat is supplied to 100 g of water. Take c = 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹.

Step 1: Convert mass: 100 g = 0.1 kg

Step 2: Use Q = mcΔT

Step 3: ΔT = Q/(mc) = 500/(0.1 × 4200)

Step 4: ΔT = 1.19 ≈ 1.2°C

Answer: 1.2°C
Example 2: Heat Capacity of a Body

An aluminium block is heated by a 30 W heater. Its temperature rises by 100°C in 5 minutes. Find its heat capacity.

Step 1: Time = 5 min = 300 s

Step 2: Heat supplied = Q = Pt = 30 × 300 = 9000 J

Step 3: C = Q/ΔT = 9000/100 = 90 J K⁻¹

Answer: 90 J K⁻¹
Example 3: Method of Mixtures

A metal of mass 0.5 kg at 100°C is placed in a calorimeter of heat capacity 80 J K⁻¹ containing water of heat capacity 420 J K⁻¹ at 20°C. Find the final temperature. Take c_m = 400 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹.

Let final temperature = θ

Heat lost by metal: 0.5 × 400 × (100 - θ) = 200(100 - θ)

Heat gained by calorimeter + water: 80(θ - 20) + 420(θ - 20) = 500(θ - 20)

Equate: 200(100 - θ) = 500(θ - 20)

Solve: 20000 - 200θ = 500θ - 10000

30000 = 700θ

θ = 42.9°C ≈ 43°C

Answer: about 43°C
Example 4: Finding Specific Heat Capacity of a Metal

A metal of mass 0.5 kg at 100°C is transferred into a calorimeter of heat capacity 80 J K⁻¹ with water of heat capacity 420 J K⁻¹ at 20°C. If the final temperature is 44°C, find the metal’s specific heat capacity.

Heat lost by metal: 0.5 × c × (100 - 44) = 28c

Heat gained: 80(44 - 20) + 420(44 - 20)

= 80(24) + 420(24) = 1920 + 10080 = 12000 J

Equate: 28c = 12000

c = 12000/28 = 428.6 ≈ 429 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹

Answer: about 430 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
Example 5: Electrical Heating Method

A 50 W electric heater is immersed in a liquid of mass 0.5 kg. Its temperature rises from 25°C to 75°C in 210 s. Find the liquid’s specific heat capacity. Assume no heat loss.

Step 1: ΔT = 75 - 25 = 50°C

Step 2: Heat supplied = Q = Pt = 50 × 210 = 10500 J

Step 3: c = Q/(mΔT) = 10500/(0.5 × 50)

Step 4: c = 420 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹

Answer: 420 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
Example 6: Comparing Two Substances

Two liquids X and Y of equal mass receive the same quantity of heat. The temperature rise of X is twice that of Y. Find c_X : c_Y.

Use Q = mcΔT

Since m and Q are the same, c is inversely proportional to ΔT.

If ΔT_X = 2ΔT_Y, then c_X = 1/2 c_Y

Answer: 1:2
Example 7: Heating and Melting Ice

Calculate the heat required to melt 1 kg of ice initially at -30°C. Take L = 3.5×10⁵ J kg⁻¹ and c_ice = 2.1×10³ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹.

Stage 1: Warm ice from -30°C to 0°C

Q₁ = mcΔT = 1 × 2100 × 30 = 63000 J

Stage 2: Melt ice at 0°C

Q₂ = mL = 1 × 3.5×10⁵ = 350000 J

Total: Q = Q₁ + Q₂ = 413000 J = 4.13×10⁵ J

Answer: 4.13 × 10⁵ J

8️⃣ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to convert mass from grams to kilograms
  • Leaving time in minutes instead of seconds in electrical heating questions
  • Confusing heat capacity C with specific heat capacity c
  • Mixing up heat and temperature
  • For mixture questions, failing to identify which body loses heat and which gains heat
🧠 Quick check

In method of mixtures, always start from: heat lost = heat gained.

9️⃣ JAMB Exam Tips

How to score well here

  • Convert all units first before using formulas
  • Remember water = 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
  • Most metals lie roughly between 400 and 900 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
  • For electrical heating, use Q = Pt = IVt
  • For latent heat questions, break the process into stages
