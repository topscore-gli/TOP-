PHYSICS

💨 Gas Laws

JAMB Physics • Boyle’s law • Charles’ law • Pressure law • ideal gas equation • absolute zero

🎯 Learning Objective

This chapter covers the main gas laws, absolute zero, the combined gas equation, the ideal gas equation, and the Van der Waals correction for real gases.

  • Boyle’s law links pressure and volume
  • Charles’ law links volume and absolute temperature
  • Pressure law links pressure and absolute temperature
  • Temperature must usually be in Kelvin
🧠 Big idea

Gas laws describe how pressure, volume, and temperature of a gas change when one or more of them are kept constant.

1️⃣ Boyle’s Law

The pressure of a fixed mass of gas is inversely proportional to its volume when temperature remains constant.

Boyle’s law formulas
P ∝ 1/V
PV = constant
P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
  • Temperature must stay constant
  • This is called an isothermal process
  • As pressure increases, volume decreases
Boyle’s law graph idea
For Boyle’s law, the graph of pressure against volume is a falling curve.

2️⃣ Charles’ Law

The volume of a fixed mass of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature when pressure is constant.

Charles’ law formulas
V ∝ T
V/T = constant
V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

Important: temperature must be in Kelvin.

Temperature conversion
K = °C + 273

3️⃣ Pressure Law

The pressure of a fixed mass of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature when volume is constant.

Pressure law formulas
P ∝ T
P/T = constant
P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂
  • Volume remains constant
  • As temperature increases, pressure increases

4️⃣ Absolute Zero

Absolute zero is the theoretical temperature at which a gas would have zero volume and zero pressure by ideal extrapolation.

Absolute zero
0 K = -273°C
  • It is the lowest possible temperature
  • It is the zero point of the Kelvin scale
  • It is a major idea behind gas law graphs
🧠 Key warning

Do not confuse 0°C with 0 K. They are very different.

5️⃣ General Gas Equation

When pressure, volume, and temperature all change for a fixed mass of gas, we use the combined gas law.

General gas equation
P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

This combines Boyle’s law, Charles’ law, and the Pressure law.

6️⃣ Ideal Gas Equation

For n moles of an ideal gas:

Ideal gas equation
PV = nRT
  • P = pressure
  • V = volume
  • n = number of moles
  • R = universal gas constant
  • T = temperature in Kelvin
Alternative form
PV = (m/M)RT

At low pressure and high temperature, real gases behave more like ideal gases.

7️⃣ Van der Waals Equation

Real gases do not behave perfectly like ideal gases, so a corrected equation is used.

Van der Waals equation
(P + an²/V²)(V - nb) = nRT
  • a corrects for intermolecular forces
  • b corrects for the volume occupied by gas molecules
🧠 JAMB note

For most JAMB questions, Van der Waals is tested more as a concept than as a hard calculation.

8️⃣ Essential Calculations for JAMB

Calculation 1: Boyle’s Law

A gas occupies 200 cm³ at 760 mmHg. Find its volume at 950 mmHg if temperature is constant.

Step: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

760 × 200 = 950 × V₂

V₂ = 152000 / 950 = 160 cm³

Answer: 160 cm³
Calculation 2: Charles’ Law

A gas occupies 500 cm³ at 27°C. At what temperature will it occupy 600 cm³ at constant pressure?

Step 1: T₁ = 27 + 273 = 300 K

Step 2: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

500/300 = 600/T₂

T₂ = 360 K = 87°C

Answer: 87°C
Calculation 3: Pressure Law

A gas exerts 80 cmHg at 27°C. Find its pressure at 127°C if the volume is constant.

Step 1: T₁ = 300 K, T₂ = 400 K

Step 2: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂

80/300 = P₂/400

P₂ = 106.67 cmHg

Answer: 106.67 cmHg
Calculation 4: General Gas Equation

A gas occupies 300 cm³ at 27°C and 750 mmHg. Find its volume at -23°C and 600 mmHg.

Step 1: T₁ = 300 K, T₂ = 250 K

Step 2: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

(750 × 300)/300 = (600 × V₂)/250

750 = 600V₂/250

V₂ = 312.5 cm³

Answer: 312.5 cm³
Calculation 5: Ideal Gas at STP

Find the volume occupied by 2 moles of an ideal gas at STP. Take T = 273 K, P = 1.01×10⁵ Pa, and R = 8.314.

Step: V = nRT/P

V = (2 × 8.314 × 273)/(1.01×10⁵)

V = 0.0449 m³ ≈ 44.9 dm³

Answer: 0.0449 m³ or 44.9 dm³
Calculation 6: Finding Mass from Ideal Gas Equation

What is the mass of O₂ in a 10 dm³ container at 25°C and 200 kPa? Take molar mass = 32 g/mol.

Step 1: V = 0.01 m³, T = 298 K, P = 200000 Pa

Step 2: Use PV = (m/M)RT

m = PVM/(RT)

m = (200000 × 0.01 × 32)/(8.314 × 298)

m = 25.83 g

Answer: 25.83 g
Calculation 7: Absolute Zero Estimate

A gas has volume 400 cm³ at 0°C. If its volume decreases by 1.5 cm³ per 1°C drop, estimate absolute zero.

Step 1: Temperature drop for zero volume = 400/1.5 = 266.67°C

Step 2: Absolute zero ≈ 0 - 266.67 = -267°C

Answer: about -267°C

🔟 JAMB Exam Tips

High-score reminders

  • Always convert Celsius to Kelvin first
  • Check pressure and volume units carefully
  • STP means 273 K and 1.01 × 10⁵ Pa
  • Use the combined gas law when pressure, volume, and temperature all change
  • Real gases are more ideal at low pressure and high temperature

1️⃣1️⃣ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to add 273 when converting °C to K
  • Mixing units like mmHg, Pa, atm, cmHg carelessly
  • Using Boyle’s law when temperature is not constant
  • Confusing absolute zero with 0°C
  • Using the wrong gas law when more than one variable changes
