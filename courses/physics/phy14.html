🎯 Learning Objective

  • Temperature stays constant during change of state

🧠 Big idea

During a change of state, added heat does not raise temperature — it is used to break or form intermolecular bonds.

1️⃣ Understanding Change of State

Change of state, or phase change, is the physical transformation of matter from one state to another.

States of matter
Solid ↔ Liquid ↔ Gas

When a substance is changing state, its temperature remains constant until the whole process is complete.

For example, ice at 0°C remains at 0°C while melting until all the ice becomes water.

Basic state changes
Matter can change between solid, liquid, and gas when heat is absorbed or released.

3️⃣ Latent Heat

Latent heat is the heat absorbed or released during a change of state at constant temperature.

The word latent means hidden because the heat does not show up as temperature increase.

Type Symbol Definition Value for Water Comparison
Specific latent heat of fusion Lf Heat needed to change 1 kg of solid to liquid at melting point 336,000 J/kg Lower value
Specific latent heat of vaporization Lv Heat needed to change 1 kg of liquid to gas at boiling point 2,260,000 J/kg Much higher
← Swipe table to see more →
Master formulas
Q = mLf for melting or freezing
Q = mLv for boiling or condensation
🧠 Important comparison

For water, Lv is much greater than Lf. Vaporization needs far more heat than melting.

4️⃣ Melting, Evaporation, and Boiling

Melting and freezing

  • Melting point is the temperature at which a solid changes to liquid
  • Freezing point is the temperature at which a liquid changes to solid
  • For pure substances, melting point equals freezing point

Evaporation vs boiling

Feature Evaporation Boiling
TemperatureOccurs at any temperatureOccurs at a definite boiling point
LocationOnly at the surfaceThroughout the liquid
BubblesNo bubblesBubbles form
RateSlowRapid

Effect of pressure

← Swipe table to see more →
  • Pressure cookers raise pressure, so water boils above 100°C
  • At high altitudes, atmospheric pressure is lower, so water boils below 100°C

Effect of dissolved substances

  • Adding salt or sugar raises the boiling point
  • Adding impurities lowers the melting point or freezing point
🧠 Easy memory

Impurities usually raise boiling point but lower melting point.

7️⃣ Solved Examples for JAMB

Example 1: Latent Heat of Fusion

Calculate the heat required to melt 0.5 kg of ice at 0°C. Take Lf = 336,000 J/kg.

Step: Q = mLf = 0.5 × 336,000 = 168,000 J

Answer: 168,000 J
Example 2: Mass from Latent Heat

How much steam at 100°C is produced when 11,300 J of heat is supplied to boiling water? Take Lv = 2,260,000 J/kg.

Step: m = Q/Lv = 11,300 / 2,260,000 = 0.005 kg

Answer: 0.005 kg or 5 g
Example 3: Ice to Steam

Calculate the total heat required to change 2 kg of ice at 0°C to steam at 100°C. Take Lf = 336,000 J/kg, Lv = 2,260,000 J/kg, and c = 4200 J/kg·K.

Stage 1: Melt the ice

Q₁ = 2 × 336,000 = 672,000 J

Stage 2: Heat water from 0°C to 100°C

Q₂ = mcΔT = 2 × 4200 × 100 = 840,000 J

Stage 3: Boil water at 100°C

Q₃ = 2 × 2,260,000 = 4,520,000 J

Total: Q = Q₁ + Q₂ + Q₃ = 6,032,000 J

Answer: 6.032 × 10⁶ J
Example 4: Condensation

Calculate the heat released when 10 g of steam at 100°C condenses to water at 100°C. Take Lv = 2,260,000 J/kg.

Step 1: m = 10 g = 0.01 kg

Step 2: Q = mLv = 0.01 × 2,260,000 = 22,600 J

Answer: 22,600 J

8️⃣ Key Points to Remember for JAMB

Fast revision notes

  • Temperature stays constant during phase change
  • Lv > Lf for the same substance
  • Water has very high latent heats
  • Sublimation skips the liquid phase
  • Pressure cooker works by increasing pressure and boiling point
  • Impurities raise boiling point but lower melting point
