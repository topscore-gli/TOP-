PHYSICS

🫧 Liquids at Rest

JAMB Physics • density • relative density • upthrust • buoyancy • Archimedes’ principle • floatation

🎯 Learning Objective

This chapter explains how stationary liquids behave, why bodies float or sink, and how ideas like density and upthrust apply to ships, hydrometers, submarines, and balloons.

  • Density tells how compact matter is
  • Relative density compares a substance with water
  • Upthrust is the upward force from a fluid
  • Floating happens when weight equals upthrust
🧠 Big idea

If a body displaces enough fluid to balance its own weight, it floats. If not, it sinks.

1️⃣ Density of Solids and Liquids

Density is the mass per unit volume of a substance.

Density formula
ρ = m/V
  • SI unit: kg/m³
  • Another common unit: g/cm³
  • 1 g/cm³ = 1000 kg/m³
  • Density does not depend on the amount of substance

Water density = 1 g/cm³ = 1000 kg/m³. This is the main reference point in many JAMB questions.

Determining density

For Solids For Liquids
• Measure mass using a balance
• For regular shapes, calculate volume using formula
• For irregular solids, use water displacement
• Then use ρ = m/V 		• Measure empty container mass
• Measure container + liquid mass
• Subtract to get mass of liquid
• Measure volume and use ρ = m/V

2️⃣ Relative Density

Relative density, also called specific gravity, is the ratio of the density of a substance to the density of water.

Relative density formulas
R.D. = ρ(substance) / ρ(water)
R.D. = mass of substance / mass of equal volume of water
  • Relative density has no unit
  • For water, R.D. = 1
  • If R.D. < 1, the body floats in water
  • If R.D. > 1, the body sinks in water
🧠 Quick memory

Density has units. Relative density has no units.

Link with water density
ρ(substance) = R.D. × ρ(water)

3️⃣ Upthrust or Buoyant Force

Upthrust is the upward force exerted by a fluid on a body immersed in it.

Why upthrust happens

  • Pressure increases with depth
  • The bottom of an immersed body experiences more pressure than the top
  • This pressure difference produces a net upward force
Upthrust formula
U = ρ(fluid) × V(displaced) × g
Why upthrust acts upward
The greater pressure at the bottom gives a larger upward force than the downward force at the top.

4️⃣ Archimedes’ Principle

When a body is completely or partially immersed in a fluid, it experiences an upthrust equal to the weight of the fluid displaced.

Archimedes’ principle
Upthrust = weight of displaced fluid
U = ρ(fluid) × V(displaced) × g

Experimental verification

  • Measure weight of object in air: W(air)
  • Measure weight of object immersed in water: W(apparent)
  • Upthrust = W(air) - W(apparent)
  • Collect displaced water and weigh it
  • The two values are equal

Apparent loss in weight = Upthrust

5️⃣ Law of Floatation

A body floats in a fluid when its weight equals the weight of the fluid displaced.

Condition for floatation
Weight of body = Upthrust
m(body)g = ρ(fluid)V(submerged)g
Condition Result
Weight < maximum upthrustBody floats
Weight = upthrustBody is fully immersed or neutrally buoyant
Weight > maximum upthrustBody sinks

Fraction submerged
Fraction submerged = R.D. of body / R.D. of fluid

For ice with R.D. = 0.92 in water:

Fraction submerged = 0.92/1 = 0.92

Floating body
A floating body settles at the level where its weight equals the upthrust from the liquid.

6️⃣ Applications

Ships

  • Steel is denser than water, but ships contain hollow spaces
  • Their overall average density becomes less than that of water
  • They displace a large volume of water and get enough upthrust
  • Plimsoll lines show safe loading limits

Hydrometers

  • Used to measure relative density of liquids
  • It floats deeper in less dense liquids
  • It floats higher in denser liquids

Submarines

  • Ballast tanks fill with water to increase weight and sink
  • Water is pumped out to reduce weight and rise
  • Neutral buoyancy occurs when weight equals upthrust

Hot air balloons

  • Hot air is less dense than cold air
  • If total weight is less than the weight of displaced air, the balloon rises

7️⃣ Worked Examples for JAMB

Example 1: Upthrust, Volume, Density, and R.D.

A solid weighs 0.50 N in air and 0.40 N in water. Find: (a) upthrust (b) volume (c) density (d) relative density.

(a) Upthrust = 0.50 - 0.40 = 0.10 N

(b) V = U/(ρg) = 0.10/(1000×10) = 1.0×10⁻⁵ m³ = 10 cm³

(c) Mass = 0.50/10 = 0.05 kg = 50 g

ρ = 50/10 = 5.0 g/cm³

(d) R.D. = 5.0/1.0 = 5.0

Answers: 0.10 N, 10 cm³, 5.0 g/cm³, 5.0
Example 2: Floating Wood

A wood block has volume 100 cm³ and density 0.6 g/cm³. Find the volume above water.

Step 1: Fraction submerged = 0.6/1.0 = 0.6

Step 2: Volume submerged = 0.6 × 100 = 60 cm³

Step 3: Volume above = 100 - 60 = 40 cm³

Answer: 40 cm³
Example 3: Object in Unknown Liquid

An object weighs 2.5 N in air and 2.0 N in a liquid of density 800 kg/m³. Find the object’s density.

Step 1: Upthrust = 2.5 - 2.0 = 0.5 N

Step 2: V = 0.5/(800×10) = 6.25×10⁻⁵ m³

Step 3: Mass = 2.5/10 = 0.25 kg

Step 4: ρ = 0.25 / 6.25×10⁻⁵ = 4000 kg/m³

Answer: 4000 kg/m³
Example 4: Finding R.D. of a Liquid

A solid weighs 10.0 N in air, 8.0 N in water, and 9.0 N in another liquid. Find the liquid’s relative density.

Step 1: Upthrust in water = 10.0 - 8.0 = 2.0 N

Step 2: Upthrust in liquid = 10.0 - 9.0 = 1.0 N

Step 3: R.D. = U(liquid)/U(water) = 1.0/2.0 = 0.5

Answer: 0.5
Example 5: Tension in a Suspended Cube

A metal cube of side 5 cm and relative density 8.0 is suspended with half its volume in oil of relative density 0.8. Find the thread tension.

Step 1: Volume = 5³ = 125 cm³ = 125×10⁻⁶ m³

Step 2: Density of cube = 8000 kg/m³

Step 3: Mass = 8000 × 125×10⁻⁶ = 1.0 kg

Step 4: Weight = 1.0 × 10 = 10 N

Step 5: Immersed volume = 62.5×10⁻⁶ m³

Step 6: Upthrust = 800 × 62.5×10⁻⁶ × 10 = 0.5 N

Step 7: Tension = 10 - 0.5 = 9.5 N

Answer: 9.5 N
Example 6: Maximum Load for a Boat

A boat has volume 20 m³ and mass 8000 kg. Find: (a) maximum mass it can carry safely (b) volume displaced when fully loaded.

Step 1: Maximum upthrust = 1000 × 20 × 10 = 200,000 N

Step 2: Weight of boat = 8000 × 10 = 80,000 N

Step 3: Extra load weight = 200,000 - 80,000 = 120,000 N

(a) Maximum mass = 120,000/10 = 12,000 kg

(b) Fully loaded means displaced volume = 20 m³

Answers: 12,000 kg and 20 m³
Example 7: Iceberg Volume

An iceberg of density 920 kg/m³ has 2000 m³ above sea water. Take sea water density as 1030 kg/m³. Find its total volume.

Step 1: Fraction submerged = 920/1030 = 0.893

Step 2: Fraction above = 1 - 0.893 = 0.107

Step 3: 0.107V = 2000

Step 4: V = 2000/0.107 ≈ 18,700 m³

Answer: about 18,700 m³
Example 8: Relative Density of a Liquid

A solid weighs 0.080 N in air and 0.060 N in a liquid. If the solid’s R.D. is 4.0, find the liquid’s R.D.

Step 1: Density of solid = 4000 kg/m³

Step 2: Mass = 0.080/10 = 0.008 kg

Step 3: Volume = 0.008/4000 = 2×10⁻⁶ m³

Step 4: Upthrust = 0.080 - 0.060 = 0.020 N

Step 5: ρ(liquid) = 0.020/(2×10⁻⁶×10) = 1000 kg/m³

Step 6: Relative density = 1000/1000 = 1.0

Answer: 1.0

9️⃣ Study Tips for JAMB

How to avoid mistakes

  • Master the formulas because JAMB asks many calculations here
  • Do not confuse density with relative density
  • Remember 1 g/cm³ = 1000 kg/m³
  • For floating bodies, always use Weight = Upthrust
  • If R.D. is less than 1, the body floats in water
  • If R.D. is greater than 1, it sinks in water

You should be able to:

  • Distinguish between density and relative density
  • Determine upthrust on immersed bodies
  • Apply Archimedes’ principle
  • Apply the law of floatation
  • Solve problems on floating and sinking bodies
