🧲 Magnets and Magnetic Fields

JAMB Physics • natural magnets • artificial magnets • soft iron and steel • field patterns • Earth’s field • flux • flux density

🎯 Learning Objective

This chapter explains magnets, magnetic materials, methods of magnetization and demagnetization, magnetic field patterns, Earth's magnetic field, magnetic flux, and flux density for JAMB success.

  • Magnets always have two poles: North and South
  • Like poles repel, unlike poles attract
  • Soft iron is for temporary magnets, steel for permanent magnets
  • Magnetic field lines form continuous closed loops
🧠 Big idea

Magnetic effects come from magnets or electric currents, and they can be studied using field lines, force rules, and flux formulas.

3️⃣ Methods of Making and Demagnetizing Magnets

Making magnets

  • Stroking method: stroke with one pole repeatedly in one direction
  • Divided touch: two like poles stroke from the center outward
  • Electrical method: place material in a solenoid and pass direct current
  • Induction: place unmagnetized iron near a strong magnet

4️⃣ Concept of Magnetic Field

A magnetic field is the region around a magnet or current-carrying conductor where magnetic force can be detected.

  • It is a vector quantity
  • Its direction is shown by magnetic field lines
  • Outside a magnet, lines go from North to South
  • Inside a magnet, lines go from South to North

Properties of field lines

  • They never cross one another
  • They are crowded near the poles
  • They form closed loops
  • The tangent at any point gives the direction of the field

6️⃣ Magnetic Field Around Current-Carrying Conductors

A. Straight conductor

The magnetic field forms concentric circles around the wire.

Field around a straight wire
Use the right-hand grip rule to find the field direction.

B. Circular coil

The field pattern is similar to that of a short bar magnet and is strongest near the center.

C. Solenoid

A solenoid behaves like a bar magnet.

🧠 Polarity rule

If current appears clockwise at one end, that end is South. If anticlockwise, that end is North.

Solenoid as a magnet
A long current-carrying solenoid produces a bar-magnet-like field.

Calculation 4: Earth’s Field Components

The Earth’s total magnetic flux density at a place is 5.0 × 10⁻⁵ T and the angle of dip is 60°. Find the horizontal and vertical components.

Bₕ = B cos δ = 5.0 × 10⁻⁵ × 0.5 = 2.5 × 10⁻⁵ T

Bᵥ = B sin δ = 5.0 × 10⁻⁵ × 0.866 = 4.33 × 10⁻⁵ T

Answer: 2.5 × 10⁻⁵ T and 4.33 × 10⁻⁵ T

Calculation 1: Magnetic Flux

A rectangular coil of dimensions 20 cm by 15 cm is placed perpendicular to a magnetic field of 0.4 T. Find the flux.

A = 0.20 × 0.15 = 0.03 m²

Φ = BA = 0.4 × 0.03 = 0.012 Wb

Answer: 0.012 Wb
Calculation 2: Force on a Conductor

A 50 cm wire carries 4.0 A and is perpendicular to a field of 0.60 T. Find the force.

F = BIL = 0.60 × 4.0 × 0.50 = 1.2 N

Answer: 1.2 N
Calculation 3: Force at an Angle

A conductor 1.4 m long carries 0.87 A and is at 30° to a field of 80 mT. Find the force.

B = 0.080 T

F = BIL sin θ = 0.080 × 0.87 × 1.4 × 0.5 = 0.049 N

Answer: 0.049 N
Calculation 5: Flux Linkage

A solenoid has 300 turns and radius 0.40 m. It is in a field of 5.1 mT perpendicular to its cross-section. Find the flux linkage.

A = πr² = π(0.40)² = 0.503 m²

Φ = BA = 5.1 × 10⁻³ × 0.503 = 2.565 × 10⁻³ Wb

NΦ = 300 × 2.565 × 10⁻³ = 0.77 Wb-turns

Answer: 0.77 Wb-turns
Calculation 6: Finding Flux Density

A 25 cm wire carrying 8.0 A experiences a force of 0.40 N when perpendicular to a magnetic field. Find the flux density.

B = F/(IL) = 0.40/(8.0 × 0.25) = 0.20 T

Answer: 0.20 T

9️⃣ Variation of Magnetic Field Intensity

  • Earth’s magnetic field is stronger near the poles
  • It is weaker near the equator
  • It changes slowly with time
  • It decreases with altitude
  • Local minerals can cause magnetic anomalies

At the magnetic poles, dip angle is 90° and the horizontal component is zero. At the magnetic equator, dip angle is 0° and the vertical component is zero.
