🎵 Characteristics of Sound Waves

JAMB Physics • pitch • loudness • quality • echo • Doppler effect • resonance • beats

🎯 Learning Objective

This chapter explains the nature of sound waves, pitch, loudness, quality, speed of sound, echo, Doppler effect, resonance in pipes, and beat frequency calculations.

  • Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave
  • Pitch depends on frequency
  • Loudness depends on amplitude
  • Beat frequency is the difference between two close frequencies
🧠 Big idea

Different characteristics of sound depend on different wave properties: frequency gives pitch, amplitude gives loudness, and waveform gives quality.

1️⃣ Nature of Sound Waves

Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave that needs a material medium to travel.

  • Compression: region of high pressure and density
  • Rarefaction: region of low pressure and density
Longitudinal sound wave
Sound moves as compressions and rarefactions through a medium.

Sound cannot travel through vacuum because there are no particles there to vibrate.

2️⃣ Characteristics of Sound Waves

A. Pitch

Pitch depends on the frequency of the sound wave.

  • Higher frequency → higher pitch
  • Lower frequency → lower pitch
  • Human hearing range is about 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz
High pitch vs low pitch
Closer wave cycles mean higher frequency and higher pitch.

B. Loudness

Loudness depends on the amplitude of the sound wave.

  • Greater amplitude → louder sound
  • Measured in decibels (dB)
Loud sound vs soft sound
Amplitude affects loudness, not pitch.

C. Quality (Timbre)

Quality allows us to distinguish between two sounds of the same pitch and loudness.

It depends on the waveform and the relative intensities of overtones or harmonics.

🧠 Quick memory

Pitch = frequency, Loudness = amplitude, Quality = waveform/overtones.

3️⃣ Speed of Sound

The speed of sound depends on the medium and, in gases, on the temperature.

Speed in gases
v = √(γRT/M)
Temperature relationship
v ∝ √T
v₁/v₂ = √(T₁/T₂)
  • Sound travels fastest in solids
  • Then in liquids
  • Slowest in gases

For JAMB, speed of sound in air is often taken as about 330–340 m/s.

4️⃣ Echo and Reverberation

Echo

An echo is the reflection of sound from a distant surface and is heard as a distinct repetition.

Echo distance formula
d = vt/2
  • Minimum distance for a distinct echo is about 17 m
  • The factor 2 is for the forward and return journey of sound
Echo from a wall
The reflected sound returns to the listener as an echo.

Reverberation

Reverberation is caused by multiple reflections that make sound persist for a short time in a hall or room.

5️⃣ Doppler Effect

The Doppler effect is the apparent change in frequency due to relative motion between the source and the observer.

Scenario Observed Frequency
Approaching sourceHigher frequency
Receding sourceLower frequency

Moving source
f' = f[v/(v ± vₛ)]
Moving observer
f' = f[(v ± v₀)/v]
🧠 Sign reminder

Approaching gives higher frequency. For a moving source, use minus in the denominator when approaching and plus when receding.

6️⃣ Resonance and Stationary Waves in Pipes

Open pipes

For a pipe open at both ends:

Open pipe formulas
f₀ = v/2L
fₙ = nf₀

All harmonics are present.

Closed pipes

For a pipe closed at one end:

Closed pipe formulas
f₀ = v/4L
fₙ = nf₀, n = 1, 3, 5...

Only odd harmonics are present.

Open pipe vs closed pipe
Open pipes support all harmonics, while closed pipes support only odd harmonics.

7️⃣ Beats

When two sounds of slightly different frequencies are heard together, beats are produced.

Beat frequency
fbeat = |f₁ - f₂|
  • Number of beats per second equals the difference in frequencies
  • Useful in tuning musical instruments

8️⃣ Essential Calculations for JAMB

Calculation 1: Speed and Wavelength

A sound wave of frequency 500 Hz travels through air at 340 m/s. Find the wavelength and period.

Wavelength: λ = v/f = 340/500 = 0.68 m

Period: T = 1/f = 1/500 = 0.002 s = 2 ms

Answer: 0.68 m and 2 ms
Calculation 2: Echo

A man stands 510 m from a cliff. How long will it take to hear the echo if v = 340 m/s?

Step: Total distance = 2 × 510 = 1020 m

t = 1020/340 = 3 s

Answer: 3 s
Calculation 3: Temperature Effect on Speed

Speed of sound is 330 m/s at 0°C (273 K). Find the speed at 27°C (300 K).

Step: v₁/v₂ = √(T₁/T₂)

330/v₂ = √(273/300)

v₂ ≈ 346 m/s

Answer: 346 m/s
Calculation 4: Doppler Effect

A train moving at 30 m/s sounds a whistle of frequency 500 Hz. Find the frequency heard by a stationary observer as the train approaches. Take v = 340 m/s.

Step: f' = f[v/(v - vₛ)]

f' = 500 × 340/(340 - 30) = 548.4 Hz

Answer: 548.4 Hz
Calculation 5: Resonance in Open Pipe

An open pipe is 0.5 m long. Find its fundamental frequency if v = 340 m/s.

Step: f₀ = v/2L = 340/(2 × 0.5) = 340 Hz

Answer: 340 Hz
Calculation 6: Beats

Two tuning forks produce 5 beats per second. One fork has frequency 256 Hz. Find the possible frequencies of the second fork.

Step: 5 = |256 - f₂|

f₂ = 261 Hz or 251 Hz

Answer: 261 Hz or 251 Hz
Calculation 7: Closed Pipe

A closed pipe of length 0.4 m resonates at its fundamental frequency. Find the frequency if v = 320 m/s.

Step: f₀ = v/4L = 320/(4 × 0.4) = 200 Hz

Answer: 200 Hz

🔟 JAMB Exam Tips

High-score reminders

  • Use about 330–340 m/s for sound in air unless another value is given
  • In echo problems, always remember the sound goes and comes back
  • Open pipes have all harmonics, closed pipes have only odd harmonics
  • Approaching Doppler cases give higher observed frequency
  • Convert temperatures to Kelvin in temperature-ratio formulas

1️⃣1️⃣ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting the factor of 2 in echo calculations
  • Using Celsius instead of Kelvin in temperature-ratio problems
  • Mixing up open pipe and closed pipe formulas
  • Using the wrong sign in Doppler effect
  • Thinking beat frequency is the average instead of the difference
