📋 Quick Overview

Friction is a force that opposes motion or the tendency of motion between two surfaces in contact.

It acts parallel to the surfaces and opposite to the direction of motion.

If an object wants to move to the right, friction acts to the left.

  • Friction opposes motion
  • It acts along the surface
  • It can help or hinder motion
🧠 Easy picture

Think of friction as a force that says, “I don’t want this surface to slide easily.”

1️⃣ Static and Dynamic (Kinetic) Friction

Static friction

This is the friction that prevents an object from starting to move.

When you apply a small force to a block and it does not move, static friction is acting.

Maximum static friction
Fmax = μsN
μs = coefficient of static friction
N = normal reaction

Static friction adjusts itself to match the applied force until it reaches a maximum value.

Dynamic or kinetic friction

This acts when the object is already sliding.

It is usually smaller than the maximum static friction for the same surfaces.

Kinetic friction
Fk = μkN
μk = coefficient of kinetic friction

Very important JAMB fact

  • μs > μk in most cases
  • So it is harder to start motion than to keep motion going

2️⃣ Coefficient of Limiting Friction

The coefficient of limiting friction is the ratio of maximum frictional force to normal reaction.

Formula
μ = fmax / N

It has no unit because it is a ratio.

It depends on the nature of the surfaces in contact.

  • Rough surfaces → larger μ
  • Smooth surfaces → smaller μ
  • It is dimensionless

3️⃣ How to Determine Coefficient of Friction Experimentally

A common method is the inclined plane method.

  1. Place the object on an inclined plane.
  2. Increase the angle gradually.
  3. Note the angle θ at which the object just begins to slide.

At that limiting angle:

Inclined plane relation
mg sinθ = μ mg cosθ
μ = tanθ

The angle where the body just starts to slide is called the angle of repose.

🧠 Quick memory rule

For inclined plane friction questions, once the body is just about to move, think immediately: μ = tanθ.

4️⃣ Advantages and Disadvantages of Friction

Advantages

  • It enables walking without slipping
  • It allows vehicles to brake and turn
  • It helps us hold objects firmly
  • It allows writing with pen or pencil
  • It helps belts and pulleys transmit motion

Disadvantages

  • It causes wear and tear of machine parts
  • It wastes energy as heat
  • It reduces efficiency of machines
  • It increases fuel consumption in vehicles
  • It can cause overheating

Exam shortcut

  • If friction helps movement or grip → advantage
  • If friction wastes energy or damages parts → disadvantage

5️⃣ Reduction of Friction

Since friction can be harmful in machines, we often reduce it.

  • Lubrication — use oil or grease between surfaces
  • Ball bearings or rollers — change sliding friction to rolling friction
  • Polishing surfaces — makes surfaces smoother
  • Streamlining — reduces air or fluid resistance
  • Air cushions or magnetic levitation — reduce direct contact

Important: Rolling friction is much smaller than sliding friction.

6️⃣ Viscosity

Viscosity is the internal friction in a fluid.

It is the property that resists the flow of one layer of fluid over another.

Easy idea

Honey has high viscosity, so it flows slowly.

Water has low viscosity, so it flows easily.

Factors affecting viscosity

  • Nature of the fluid
  • Temperature
🧠 Important temperature rule

For liquids, viscosity usually decreases when temperature increases.

7️⃣ Terminal Velocity

When an object falls through a fluid, it first accelerates because of gravity.

As its speed increases, drag force increases too.

Eventually:

At terminal velocity
Weight = Drag + Buoyancy
Net force = 0
Acceleration = 0

The object then moves with constant speed called terminal velocity.

  • No further acceleration at terminal velocity
  • Heavier objects may have larger terminal velocity
  • More viscous fluids reduce terminal velocity

8️⃣ Stoke’s Law

Stoke’s law gives the viscous drag on a small sphere moving slowly through a viscous fluid.

Stoke’s law
Fd = 6πηrv
η = viscosity of fluid
r = radius of sphere
v = velocity

At terminal velocity:

Force balance
Weight − Buoyancy − Drag = 0
Terminal velocity formula
vt = 2r²g(ρ − σ) / 9η
ρ = density of sphere
σ = density of fluid

Direct proportionalities to remember

  • vt ∝ r²
  • vt ∝ (ρ − σ)
  • vt ∝ 1/η

🔟 10 JAMB-Style Examples with Step-by-Step Solutions

1. An object of mass 80 kg is pulled on a horizontal rough ground by a force of 500 N just to start motion. Find the coefficient of static friction. (g = 10 m/s²)

A. 0.625 B. 0.5 C. 0.4 D. 0.8

Step 1: Normal reaction, N = mg = 80 × 10 = 800 N

Step 2: At limiting motion, F = μsN

Step 3: 500 = μs × 800

Step 4: μs = 500/800 = 0.625

Answer: A. 0.625
2. A body of mass 36 kg falls through a viscous liquid which offers a drag force of 260 N when at terminal velocity. Find the upthrust. (g = 10 m/s²)

A. 360 N B. 100 N C. 260 N D. 0 N

Step 1: Weight = mg = 36 × 10 = 360 N

Step 2: At terminal velocity, Weight = Drag + Upthrust

Step 3: 360 = 260 + U

Step 4: U = 100 N

Answer: B. 100 N
3. The coefficient of friction between a road and tire is 0.4. Find the maximum frictional force on a car of mass 1000 kg. (g = 10 m/s²)

A. 4000 N B. 1000 N C. 400 N D. 10000 N

Step 1: N = mg = 1000 × 10 = 10,000 N

Step 2: Friction = μN = 0.4 × 10,000

Step 3: Friction = 4000 N

Answer: A. 4000 N
4. A block on a horizontal surface requires 50 N to start moving and 40 N to keep moving at constant speed. Find μs and μk if mass = 20 kg. (g = 10 m/s²)

A. 0.25, 0.20 B. 0.20, 0.25 C. 0.5, 0.4 D. 2.5, 2.0

Step 1: N = mg = 20 × 10 = 200 N

Step 2: μs = 50/200 = 0.25

Step 3: μk = 40/200 = 0.20

Answer: A. 0.25, 0.20
5. A sphere falls through a viscous fluid and reaches terminal velocity. If viscosity doubles, what happens to terminal velocity?

A. Doubles B. Halves C. Quadruples D. Remains same

Step 1: vt ∝ 1/η

Step 2: If viscosity doubles, terminal velocity halves

Answer: B. Halves
6. According to Stoke’s law, drag force F is directly proportional to?

A. r B. r² C. v D. v²

Step 1: Stoke’s law: F = 6πηrv

Step 2: F is directly proportional to v

Answer: C. v
7. A steel ball falls in oil. If radius doubles, terminal velocity becomes?

A. Half B. Double C. 4 times D. 1/4 times

Step 1: vt ∝ r²

Step 2: If r becomes 2r, then vt becomes 4 times

Answer: C. 4 times
8. Two spheres of the same material fall in the same fluid. Radius of one is twice the other. Find ratio of terminal velocities (larger : smaller).

A. 1:1 B. 2:1 C. 4:1 D. 1:4

Step 1: vt ∝ r²

Step 2: Ratio = (2r)² : r² = 4:1

Answer: C. 4:1
9. A raindrop reaches terminal velocity because

A. Weight = drag B. Acceleration increases C. Viscosity zero D. Buoyancy zero

Step 1: At terminal velocity, net force is zero

Step 2: For a small drop, weight is approximately balanced by drag

Answer: A. Weight = drag
10. A copper ball of radius 2 mm and density 8.9 × 10³ kg/m³ has terminal velocity 6.5 cm/s in oil of density 1.5 × 10³ kg/m³. Find the viscosity of oil. (g = 10 m/s²)

A. 1.01 Pa·s B. 2.0 Pa·s C. 0.5 Pa·s D. 0.1 Pa·s

Step 1: Convert values: r = 0.002 m, vt = 0.065 m/s

Step 2: Use vt = 2r²g(ρ − σ)/9η

Step 3: 0.065 = 2(0.002)²(10)(8900 − 1500)/9η

Step 4: 0.065 = 0.592 / 9η

Step 5: η = 0.592 / (9 × 0.065) ≈ 1.01 Pa·s

Answer: A. 1.01 Pa·s
