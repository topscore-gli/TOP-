🪨 Soil (Ecology) - JAMB Study Guide

Soil types, components, fertility, and conservation methods (with simple experiments)

🎯 Overview

Soil supports plant growth and affects ecosystems and agriculture. JAMB tests soil types, simple experiments, and how to maintain soil fertility.

Soil profile: topsoil (most fertile) is the key layer for crops

What JAMB Wants You To Do

  • Compare sandy, loamy, clayey soils
  • Explain soil components (air, water, minerals, humus, organisms)
  • Define soil fertility and explain how it is lost
  • Apply conservation methods (rotation, mulching, terracing, etc.)
  • Describe simple experiments (jar test, drainage test, capillarity test)

🟤 a. Characteristics of Soil Types

Definition (Soil texture): Soil texture is the size of soil particles (sand, silt, clay). Texture affects drainage, aeration, and fertility.

Soil texture depends on the proportion of sand, silt, and clay

Soil Type Particle Size Feel/Texture Porosity Capillarity Water Retention Humus Drainage Best for Crops?
Sandy Large Gritty, coarse High Low Low (drains fast) Low Excellent Poor (few nutrients)
Loamy Mixed Crumbly, smooth Moderate Moderate Moderate High Good Best (ideal)
Clayey Very small Sticky wet, hard dry Low High High (holds water) Moderate–High Poor Can be good but waterlogs

Key Definitions (JAMB-friendly)

  • Soil structure: how particles clump (granular = good; compact = bad)
  • Porosity: air/water spaces (high porosity = more air)
  • Capillarity: how high water rises (small pores = high capillarity)
  • Humus: decayed organic matter; dark; improves fertility
🏖️ Sandy (Fast drain)
  • Best for: crops needing good drainage
  • Problem: low nutrients + dries quickly
🍫 Clay (Holds water)
  • Best for: water-loving crops (if drained well)
  • Problem: waterlogging + poor aeration

Particle size: sand is largest, clay is smallest

Simple ways to identify soil types (JAMB loves this)

  • Rub test: sandy = gritty; clay = sticky; loam = neither extreme
  • Drainage test: equal water + equal soil → sandy drains fastest; clay holds longest
  • Jar test: shake soil in water → sand settles first, then silt, clay last
🧠 QUICK MEMORY:

Sandy = “Sand runs” (fast drainage) | Clay = “Clings” (holds water) | Loam = “Lovely” (best balance)

🧩 b. Components of Soil

Definition: Soil is a mixture of mineral particles, organic matter, air, water, and living organisms.

Good soil (average): minerals + organic matter + air + water

Average composition of good soil: ~45% minerals + ~5% organic matter + ~25% air + ~25% water (varies by soil and season).

Component Description Role/Importance for Plants Approx. %
Inorganic (Minerals) Sand, silt, clay (from weathered rocks) Provides structure; holds some nutrients 45–50%
Organic (Humus) Decayed plant/animal remains Improves structure; holds water/nutrients; feeds microbes 5–10%
Soil organisms Bacteria, fungi, earthworms, insects Decompose → release nutrients; improve soil structure Small but active
Soil air O₂ and CO₂ in pore spaces Roots need O₂ for respiration 20–30%
Soil water Water held in pores (gravitational/capillary/hygroscopic) Main water source + dissolves nutrients 20–30%

🌱 c. Soil Fertility

Definition: Soil fertility is the ability of soil to supply nutrients needed for healthy plant growth and good yield.

Fertile soil supports strong roots and healthy plant growth

i. Loss of Soil Fertility (Causes)

  • Leaching: heavy rain washes nutrients (especially N, K) downward
  • Erosion: topsoil is removed by water or wind
  • Monoculture: same crop removes same nutrients repeatedly
  • Compaction: machinery/overgrazing reduces air pores
  • Loss of organic matter: little manure/compost → low humus
  • Burning/oxidation: destroys organic matter

Erosion removes topsoil (the most fertile layer)

ii. Renewal & Maintenance (Conservation Methods)

Definition (Soil conservation): Practices used to prevent soil loss and maintain fertility for sustainable agriculture.

Contour ridging slows runoff and reduces erosion on slopes

Method How It Works Main Benefit
Crop rotation Grow different crops in sequence (include legumes) Restores nutrients; reduces pests
Mulching Cover soil with leaves/grass Reduces evaporation/erosion; adds humus
Contour ridging / terracing Farming along slope lines / step-like ridges Prevents erosion; holds water
Strip cropping Alternate strips of crops and grass Traps soil; reduces wind/water erosion
Mixed cropping / poly-cropping Grow several crops together Better nutrient use; reduces total crop failure
Organic fertilizer Manure/compost (slow release) Adds humus; improves structure
Inorganic fertilizer NPK (quick release) Fast nutrient supply (can leach if overused)
Shifting cultivation Farm then allow fallow to recover Natural restoration (traditional)
Cover cropping Plant legumes/cover plants Prevents erosion; adds nitrogen
Minimum tillage Less digging/ploughing Preserves soil structure and organisms

"Loam is best because it balances Air + Water + Nutrients."

🧪 Simple Experiments (JAMB Practical Focus)

1) Jar test (particle size/texture)

Jar test: sand settles first, silt next, clay last

  • Put soil + water in a jar, shake well, allow to settle
  • Layers form: sand (bottom) → silt → clay (top)

2) Drainage / water retention test

Infiltration: sandy drains faster; clay drains slowly

  • Use equal volumes of soil + equal water
  • Sandy drains fastest, clay holds water longest

3) Capillarity test

Capillarity is higher in clay due to smaller pores

Most Common Question

  • Which soil has highest capillarity? → Clayey soil

📋 Quick Revision Tips (JAMB)

  • Memorize the soil comparison table (sandy vs loamy vs clayey)
  • Know experiments: jar test, drainage test, capillarity test
  • Link everything to plants: why loam is best
  • Common MCQ:
    • Highest capillarity → clay
    • Best for most crops → loam
    • Best drainage → sandy
    • Most erosion danger → exposed topsoil on slope
  • Maintenance methods: rotation + mulching + contour ridging
🧠 One-liner:

"Sandy drains, Clay holds, Loam grows."
