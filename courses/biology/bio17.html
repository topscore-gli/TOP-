– JAMB Topic: The Ecology of Populations

Ecology is the study of how living organisms interact with each other and their environment. In this topic, we focus on populations, which are groups of individuals of the same species living in a specific area at a specific time. We'll explore how populations grow, compete, adapt, and change over time. I'll break it down step by step, using simple language, examples, and key objectives from the JAMB syllabus. Read one section at a time, take notes, and try to visualize or draw diagrams for better understanding.

We'll cover:

  • Population density and overcrowding.
  • Adaptation for survival (including competition and its link to succession).
  • Factors affecting population sizes (biotic and abiotic).
  • Ecological succession (primary and secondary).

Step 1: Population Density and Overcrowding

What is Population Density?

  • Definition: Population density is the number of individuals (organisms) in a population per unit area or volume. It's like counting how many people live in one square kilometer of a city.
  • How to Calculate It: Density = Total number of organisms ÷ Area (or volume) they occupy.
  • Example: If there are 500 rabbits in a 2 km² field, density = 500 ÷ 2 = 250 rabbits per km².
  • Why It Matters: High density can lead to overcrowding, where resources like food, water, or space become limited.

What is Overcrowding?

  • Definition: Overcrowding happens when a population grows too large for the available resources in its habitat, leading to stress and problems.
  • Reasons for Rapid Population Changes (Especially in Humans):
    • Birth rates increase: Due to better healthcare, more babies survive.
    • Death rates decrease: Vaccines, medicine, and sanitation reduce diseases.
    • Migration: People move to areas with jobs or safety.
    • In humans: Rapid growth in places like Nigeria due to high birth rates and improved child survival.
  • Consequences of Overcrowding:
    • Shortage of food, water, and housing.
    • Spread of diseases (e.g., cholera in crowded slums).
    • Pollution and environmental damage.
    • Increased competition for jobs or resources, leading to conflicts.

JAMB Link:

  • Objective (ii): Compute density as organisms per unit area (practice with simple math examples).
  • Objective (i): Reasons for human population changes (e.g., better medicine) and consequences (e.g., slums in cities like Lagos).

Quick Tip for Learning: Imagine a classroom with 20 students vs. 100—how does space, noise, and sharing resources change? Draw a simple graph showing population growth over time.

Step 2: Adaptation for Survival

Organisms adapt (change behaviors or traits) to survive in their environment, especially when facing competition or threats.

(i) Factors That Bring About Competition

Competition: A struggle between organisms for limited resources like food, water, mates, or space.

Factors Causing It:

  • Limited resources: Not enough food or shelter for everyone.
  • Population growth: More individuals mean more demand.
  • Environmental changes: Drought reduces water, forcing sharing.

Example: In a forest, trees compete for sunlight; taller ones shade shorter ones.

(ii) Intra- and Inter-Specific Competition

  • Intra-Specific Competition: Competition within the same species (e.g., two lions fighting for the same territory or mates). It's often intense because they need the same exact resources.
  • Inter-Specific Competition: Competition between different species (e.g., lions and hyenas competing for the same prey like zebras).

How It Relates to Population:

  • High population leads to more competition, causing diseases (from close contact), food shortages, and space issues.
  • Winners survive and reproduce; losers may die or migrate.

Niche Differentiation: To reduce intra-specific competition, organisms specialize in different "niches" (roles in the ecosystem).

Example: Birds of the same species might eat insects at different tree heights—one at the top, one at the bottom—to avoid fighting.

(iii) Relationship Between Competition and Succession

Competition drives succession (gradual change in community structure over time).

Strong competitors replace weaker ones, leading to a more stable community.

Example: In a new habitat, fast-growing plants win early competition, but slower, taller plants eventually outcompete them in later stages.

JAMB Link:

  • Objective (iii): Relate population increase to diseases, food/space shortages, and both types of competition.
  • Objective (iv): Niche differentiation reduces intra-specific competition (e.g., different feeding habits in the same species).
  • Objective (v): Competition leads to succession by favoring adaptable species.

Quick Tip for Learning: Think of a soccer team (intra-specific: players competing for starting spot) vs. rival teams (inter-specific). Use flashcards for intra vs. inter.

Step 3: Factors Affecting Population Sizes

Population size isn't fixed—it fluctuates due to various factors. These can limit growth (carrying capacity: max population an area can support).

(i) Biotic Factors (Living Things)

These involve interactions with other organisms.

  • Food: Availability affects growth; shortage causes starvation.
  • Pests and Diseases: Spread faster in dense populations (e.g., locust swarms destroying crops).
  • Predation: Predators reduce prey numbers (e.g., owls eating mice).
  • Competition: As discussed, intra/inter-specific fights for resources.
  • Reproductive Ability: How fast a species breeds (e.g., rabbits reproduce quickly, elephants slowly).

Effects: High biotic pressure reduces population; balanced interactions stabilize it.

(ii) Abiotic Factors (Non-Living Things)

These are physical/chemical parts of the environment.

  • Temperature: Too hot/cold kills organisms (e.g., fish die in warm polluted rivers).
  • Space: Limited area causes overcrowding.
  • Light: Plants need it for photosynthesis; shade reduces growth.
  • Rainfall: Droughts cause water shortages; floods destroy habitats.
  • Topography: Hills/valleys affect movement and shelter.
  • Pressure: Affects aquatic life (e.g., deep-sea fish).
  • pH: Soil/water acidity affects plant growth and animal health.

Effects: Extreme abiotic conditions can wipe out populations quickly.

Interactions Between Biotic and Abiotic Factors

They often link: E.g., drought (abiotic: low rainfall) leads to food shortage (biotic: less plants), causing competition and disease spikes.

Example: In Nigeria's Sahel region, low rainfall causes crop failure, leading to human migration and animal starvation.

JAMB Link:

  • Objective (vi): Deduce effects of these factors on population size (e.g., predation reduces it, good rainfall increases it).
  • Objective (vii): Interactions like drought → food scarcity → higher disease rates.

Step 4: Ecological Succession

Succession is the predictable, orderly change in the structure of an ecological community over time, leading to a stable climax community (final stage with balanced species).

(i) Primary Succession

  • Definition: Starts on bare, lifeless areas with no soil (e.g., after volcanic eruption or glacier retreat).
  • Sequence:
    • Pioneer Stage: Hardy organisms like lichens and mosses colonize, breaking down rock into soil.
    • Early Stage: Grasses and small plants grow as soil forms.
    • Intermediate Stage: Shrubs and small trees appear, improving soil.
    • Climax Stage: Large trees dominate; stable, diverse community (e.g., a mature forest).
  • Time: Takes hundreds/thousands of years.
  • Example: New volcanic island forming life from scratch.
4"LARGE"

(ii) Secondary Succession

  • Definition: Occurs on areas with existing soil but cleared of vegetation (e.g., after fire, farming, or flood).
  • Sequence: Similar to primary but faster (soil already there).
    • Pioneer Stage: Weeds and grasses quickly regrow.
    • Early Stage: Herbs and shrubs.
    • Intermediate Stage: Young trees.
    • Climax Stage: Mature forest or grassland.
  • Time: Decades to centuries.
  • Example: Abandoned farmland in Nigeria turning back into bush, then forest.

Role of Competition in Succession: Early species compete and modify the environment, making it suitable for later species, which outcompete the pioneers.
