🫁 Respiration

JAMB Chapter 7: Energy Release & Gas Exchange

📚 A. Significance of Respiration

Definition: Respiration is the biochemical process by which living organisms break down glucose (food substances) to release energy in the form of ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate).

Key Distinction: Respiration ≠ Breathing. Breathing is just the mechanical movement of air; respiration is the chemical breakdown of food to release energy.

🔍 Why Respiration is Essential

  • Provides ATP for all life activities (movement, growth, reproduction)
  • Maintains body temperature (heat energy released)
  • Produces intermediate compounds for other metabolic processes
  • Occurs in all living cells (plants and animals)
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy (ATP) + Heat
🧠 MEMORY AID:

"Respiration = Energy Liberation" - Think of it as the power plant of the cell!

🫁 B. Respiratory Organs & Surfaces

Characteristics of Good Respiratory Surfaces

  • Thin (one cell thick) - for easy diffusion
  • Moist - gases dissolve in moisture
  • Large surface area - for efficient gas exchange
  • Rich blood supply - to transport gases
  • Concentration gradient maintained

Types of Respiratory Surfaces

🔄 C. Mechanisms of Gaseous Exchange

C(i). In Plants

Stomata Structure & Function

  • Guard cells: Kidney-shaped, contain chloroplasts
  • Stomatal pore: Opening between guard cells
  • Function: Allow CO₂ in and O₂/water vapor out

Mechanism of Opening and Closing

Condition Process Result
DAY/LIGHT Photosynthesis → Glucose produced → Water enters guard cells → Turgid STOMA OPENS (CO₂ enters, O₂ exits)
NIGHT/DARK No photosynthesis → Glucose used up → Water leaves guard cells → Flaccid STOMA CLOSES (prevents water loss)

"Light = Open (like a shop), Dark = Close" or "Sunshine = Open for business!"

Other Plant Structures

  • Lenticels: Porous tissues on woody stems for gas exchange
  • Root hairs: For soil gas exchange (oxygen intake)

C(ii). In Animals

1. Gills (Fish)

  • Structure: Gill arch → Gill filaments → Lamellae (thin plates)
  • Mechanism: Water enters mouth → passes over gills → exits through operculum
  • Counter-current exchange: Blood flows opposite to water flow → maintains concentration gradient → maximum O₂ extraction

2. Tracheal System (Insects)

  • Path: Spiracles (openings) → Trachea (tubes) → Tracheoles (end in tissues)
  • Unique feature: Direct diffusion to cells (no blood needed!)
  • Spiracles: Can open/close to reduce water loss

3. Lungs (Mammals)

  • Structure: Bronchus → Bronchioles → Alveoli (air sacs)
  • Alveoli features: 300-500 million, one cell thick, covered with capillaries, moist surface

Breathing Mechanism

Inhalation Rises/Expands Contracts (flattens) Increases Decreases

⚡ D. Aerobic Respiration

Definition: Respiration that requires oxygen to break down glucose completely.

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 38 ATP (Energy)

Two Main Stages

Stage 1: Glycolysis

  • Location: Cytoplasm
  • Process: Glucose (6C) → 2 Pyruvic acid (3C)
  • Oxygen needed? No (anaerobic stage)
  • ATP produced: 2 ATP (net gain)

Stage 2: Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle)

  • Location: Mitochondria
  • Process: Pyruvic acid → CO₂ + H₂O
  • Oxygen needed? Yes (aerobic stage)
  • ATP produced: 36 ATP
  • Key point: Oxygen is the final hydrogen acceptor forming water

Total ATP Yield

Stage ATP Produced
Glycolysis 2 ATP
Krebs Cycle 36 ATP
TOTAL 38 ATP
🧠 MEMORY AID:

"38 is Great!" - Total ATP in aerobic respiration. "Glycolysis gives 2, Krebs gives plenty!"

🦠 E. Anaerobic Respiration

Definition: Respiration that occurs without oxygen (incomplete breakdown of glucose).

🐄 In Animals
  • Glucose → Lactic acid + 2 ATP
  • Occurs in muscle cells during vigorous exercise
  • Product: Lactic acid (causes fatigue/cramps)
  • Oxygen debt must be repaid
🍞 In Yeast
  • Glucose → Ethanol + CO₂ + 2 ATP
  • Process called fermentation
  • Used in brewing and baking
  • CO₂ makes bread rise

Oxygen Debt Explained

  • When? During vigorous exercise when oxygen supply is insufficient
  • What happens? Muscles respire anaerobically, producing lactic acid
  • Repayment: Heavy breathing after exercise pays back the oxygen debt
  • Result: Lactic acid converted back to glucose/oxidized to CO₂ and water
🧠 MEMORY AID:

"Run too fast → Lactic acid last → Panting hard → Oxygen debt passed!"

Fermentation Experiment

🔍 Setup

  • Conical flask with glucose + yeast solution
  • Delivery tube into lime water
  • Warm water bath (optimum temperature)
Observation: Lime water turns milky (proves CO₂ production)
Conclusion: Yeast produces CO₂ during anaerobic respiration

🧪 F. Experimental Demonstrations

Experiment 1: To Show Heat Production

  • Setup: Germinating seeds in vacuum flask with thermometer
  • Control: Boiled (dead) seeds in another flask
  • Result: Temperature rises in flask with living seeds
  • Conclusion: Respiration produces heat

Experiment 2: To Show CO₂ Production

  • Setup: Limewater + germinating seeds in airtight container
  • Result: Limewater turns milky/cloudy
  • Conclusion: CO₂ is produced during respiration

Experiment 3: To Show O₂ Uptake

  • Setup: Seeds in tube with hydrogencarbonate indicator (red)
  • Result: Indicator changes from red to yellow (acidic)
  • Conclusion: O₂ used up, CO₂ released (increases acidity)
🧠 MEMORY AID:

"Heat = Thermometer rises" | "CO₂ = Lime water milky" | "O₂ used = Indicator yellow"

⚠️ CRITICAL JAMB EXAM TIPS

  • Aerobic vs Anaerobic: Aerobic = 38 ATP, needs O₂; Anaerobic = 2 ATP, no O₂
  • Glycolysis: Always occurs in cytoplasm, produces 2 ATP, doesn't need oxygen
  • Krebs Cycle: Occurs in mitochondria, needs oxygen, produces 36 ATP
  • Stomata: Turgid guard cells = open; Flaccid = closed
  • Oxygen Debt: Caused by anaerobic respiration in muscles, repaid by heavy breathing
  • Fermentation: Yeast produces ethanol + CO₂; Animals produce lactic acid
  • Respiratory Surfaces: Must be thin, moist, large surface area, good blood supply
  • Counter-current: Fish gills use this for maximum oxygen extraction
  • Tracheal System: Insects don't use blood for gas transport (direct to cells)
  • Yeast Uses: Brewing (alcohol), Baking (CO₂ rises bread), Biofuel (ethanol)

🎯 Likely JAMB Questions & Answers

Q1: Where does glycolysis occur and how many ATP are produced?

A: Cytoplasm; 2 ATP (net gain)

Q2: What is the role of oxygen in aerobic respiration?

A: Final hydrogen acceptor forming water; allows Krebs cycle to run

Q3: Why do we breathe heavily after vigorous exercise?

A: To repay oxygen debt (convert lactic acid to glucose/CO₂ + water)

Q4: What causes bread to rise when yeast is added?

A: CO₂ produced during anaerobic respiration (fermentation)

Q5: How do guard cells open the stomata?

A: Become turgid (water enters) → bow outward → pore opens

Q6: What is the advantage of counter-current flow in fish gills?

A: Maintains concentration gradient → maximum oxygen extraction
