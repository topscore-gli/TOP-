Understanding Diseases

Diseases are a major way the environment affects human health. We'll cover common/endemic diseases and easily transmissible ones, including their causes, spread, symptoms, and control. This ties into objectives (i), (ii), (vi), and (vii).

1.1 Common and Endemic Diseases

Definition: Endemic diseases are constantly present in a specific area, often due to local ecological conditions like climate, water, or vectors (organisms that spread disease).

Examples and Factors Favoring Spread:

  • Malaria: Caused by Plasmodium parasite, spread by female Anopheles mosquitoes. Favored by warm, humid areas with stagnant water (breeding sites). Symptoms: Fever, chills, headache. Control: Use mosquito nets, insecticides, drain water; vaccination trials ongoing.
  • Meningitis: Often bacterial (e.g., Neisseria meningitidis), spreads via respiratory droplets in dry, dusty areas like Nigeria's "meningitis belt." Symptoms: Stiff neck, fever, confusion. Control: Vaccination (e.g., MenAfriVac), avoid overcrowding.
  • Dracunculiasis (Guinea Worm Disease): Caused by Dracunculus worm via contaminated water. Endemic in rural areas with unsafe drinking water. Symptoms: Painful blister, worm emerges from skin. Control: Filter water, treat sources; nearly eradicated globally.
  • Schistosomiasis (Bilharzia): Parasitic worm (Schistosoma) from snail-infested water. Spread in areas with poor sanitation near rivers/lakes. Symptoms: Bloody urine, abdominal pain. Control: Avoid infested water, use praziquantel drug, snail control.
  • Onchocerciasis (River Blindness): Caused by Onchocerca worm, spread by blackflies near fast-flowing rivers. Symptoms: Itching, blindness. Control: Ivermectin treatment, insecticide spraying.
  • Typhoid Fever: Bacterial (Salmonella typhi) via contaminated food/water. Favored by poor hygiene in crowded areas. Symptoms: High fever, weakness. Control: Vaccination, safe water, handwashing.

Key Tip: Ecological conditions like poor drainage, overcrowding, and vectors (e.g., mosquitoes for malaria) make these diseases common. Prevention often involves breaking the transmission cycle (e.g., inoculate via vaccines to build immunity).

1.2 Easily Transmissible Diseases and Syndromes

These spread quickly person-to-person or via vectors. Focus on biology of agents/vectors for control.

  • Poliomyelitis (Polio): Viral (poliovirus), spreads via fecal-oral route in unsanitary areas. Vector/Agent: No true vector; direct contact or contaminated water. Symptoms: Paralysis, muscle weakness. Control: Oral polio vaccine (OPV), sanitation; global eradication efforts.
  • Cholera: Bacterial (Vibrio cholerae), via contaminated water/food. Agent: Bacteria produce toxins causing diarrhea. Symptoms: Severe watery diarrhea, dehydration. Control: Oral rehydration, vaccines, clean water.
  • Tuberculosis (TB): Bacterial (Mycobacterium tuberculosis), airborne via coughs. Agent: Bacteria infect lungs. Symptoms: Cough with blood, weight loss. Control: BCG vaccine, antibiotics, ventilation.

Sexually Transmitted Diseases/Syndromes (STDs/STIs):

  • Gonorrhea: Bacterial (Neisseria gonorrhoeae), via sexual contact. Symptoms: Painful urination, discharge. Control: Antibiotics, condoms.
  • Syphilis: Bacterial (Treponema pallidum), via sexual contact or mother-to-child. Symptoms: Sores, rash, organ damage if untreated. Control: Penicillin, safe sex.
  • AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome): Caused by HIV virus, weakens immune system. Spread via blood, semen, etc. Symptoms: Opportunistic infections. Control: Antiretrovirals (no cure), condoms, PrEP; no vaccine yet.

Prevention Principles: Use inoculation (single dose) or vaccination (builds immunity). Control vectors (e.g., flies for onchocerciasis) and modes (e.g., hygiene for cholera). For epidemics, trace contacts and isolate.

Step 2: Drug Abuse

This section covers addiction, common drugs, effects, and prevention (objectives iii, iv, v).

2.1 Drug Addiction

Definition: Drug abuse is misuse of substances for non-medical reasons. Addiction is physical/psychological dependence, where the body craves the drug, leading to tolerance (needing more for effect) and withdrawal symptoms.

2.2 Commonly Abused Drugs

Categories and Examples:

  • Stimulants: Cocaine, amphetamines (e.g., methamphetamine) – increase alertness but cause heart issues.
  • Depressants: Alcohol, benzodiazepines (e.g., Valium) – slow brain activity, lead to coma.
  • Hallucinogens: LSD, psilocybin mushrooms – alter perception.
  • Opioids: Heroin, codeine, tramadol – pain relief but highly addictive.
  • Cannabis: Marijuana – relaxes but can impair memory.
  • Others: Inhalants (e.g., glue), prescription drugs like opioids.

2.3 Effects on Health

Short-term: Euphoria, but risks like overdose, accidents.

Long-term: Liver damage (alcohol), heart disease (cocaine), mental health issues (depression, psychosis), weakened immunity, organ failure.

Social: Crime, family breakdown.

2.4 Prevention

Education on risks, counseling, laws against illegal drugs, rehab programs. Avoid peer pressure; promote healthy alternatives like sports.

Step 3: Pollution and Its Control

Pollution harms health and ecosystems. Cover types, sources, effects, control, and sanitation (objectives viii, ix, x, xi, xii).

3.1 Sources, Types, Effects, and Control

Types:

  • Air Pollution: Gases/particles in air (e.g., CO2, smoke).
  • Water Pollution: Contaminants in water (e.g., sewage, chemicals).
  • Soil Pollution: Toxins in land (e.g., pesticides, waste).

Sources: Industries (factories), vehicles (exhaust), agriculture (fertilizers), households (waste).

Effects:

  • Human Health: Respiratory issues (air), cancers (chemicals), digestive problems (water).
  • Environment: Acid rain, biodiversity loss, climate change.

Control Methods:

  • Air: Use filters, electric vehicles, plant trees.
  • Water: Treat sewage, ban dumping.
  • Soil: Crop rotation, reduce pesticides.
  • General: Laws, recycling, monitoring.

3.2 Sanitation and Sewage

Importance: Prevents disease spread. Sanitation includes clean water, waste disposal; sewage is wastewater management.

Methods: Pit latrines/septic tanks in rural areas, treatment plants in cities. Personal hygiene: Handwashing, clean food.

Community Health: Reduces cholera/typhoid. Agencies like WHO (global standards), UNICEF (child health), IRCS (emergency aid), Nigerian Ministries of Health/Environment (policies, vaccinations).

Step 4: Conservation of Natural Resources

Protecting resources for sustainability (objectives xiii, xiv, xv).

Definition: Managing renewable (e.g., forests, water) and non-renewable (e.g., oil, minerals) resources.

Methods: Reforestation, recycling, sustainable farming, protected areas.

Benefits: Prevents desertification (land turning to desert, e.g., in Sahel), maintains biodiversity, ensures resources for future generations.

Bodies Responsible:

  • National: Nigerian Conservation Foundation (NCF) – education; Federal Ministry of Environment – policies; Nigeria National Parks – management.
  • International: WWF (wildlife protection), IUCN (species lists), UNEP (global programs).

Step 5: Game Reserves and National Parks

These are protected areas for wildlife (objective xvi).

Importance: Biodiversity conservation, tourism, research, prevent poaching.

Examples in Nigeria (Locations and Key Features):

  • Yankari National Park (Bauchi State): Hot springs, elephants, lions; eco-tourism.
  • Gashaka Gumti National Park (Taraba/Adamawa States): Largest in Nigeria; chimpanzees, mountains.
  • Cross River National Park (Cross River State): Gorillas, rainforests.
  • Kainji National Park (Niger/Kebbi States): Kainji Lake, hippos, antelopes.
  • Old Oyo National Park (Oyo State): Historical sites, buffaloes.
  • Chad Basin National Park (Borno/Yobe States): Wetlands, migratory birds.
  • Kamuku National Park (Kaduna State): Savanna, elephants.
  • Okomu National Park (Edo State): Forest elephants, monkeys.

Game Reserves: Similar but allow limited hunting (e.g., Borgu Game Reserve in Kainji).

Final Tips for Learning and JAMB Prep

  • Summary: Humans impact the environment through pollution and resource use, but face backlash via diseases and degradation. Control involves hygiene, conservation, and agencies.
  • Study Hacks: Make flashcards for diseases (cause, vector, control). Draw mind maps for pollution types. Memorize Nigerian parks with a map.
  • Practice: Explain how malaria spreads and control it. Discuss why conservation matters.
