– JAMB Topic: Soil

Characteristics of Different Soil Types

Soils are classified based on particle size: sandy (large particles), clayey (small particles), and loamy (a balanced mix). Each has unique properties like structure (how particles clump together), porosity (spaces between particles for air/water), capillarity (how water rises through soil), and humus content (organic matter from decayed plants/animals).

Sandy Soil

  • Particles: Large, coarse (0.05–2 mm), gritty feel
  • Structure: Loose, non-sticky, falls apart easily
  • Porosity: High (large air spaces)
  • Capillarity: Low (water rises poorly)
  • Water retention: Low (drains quickly)
  • Humus content: Low (nutrients leach easily)
  • Advantages: Good aeration, easy tillage
  • Disadvantages: Dries fast, low fertility
  • Suitable crops: Carrots, groundnuts, cowpea

Clayey Soil

  • Particles: Very small (<0.002 mm), smooth/sticky feel
  • Structure: Compact, sticky when wet, hard/cracked when dry
  • Porosity: Low (small pores)
  • Capillarity: High (water rises well)
  • Water retention: High (holds water, prone to waterlogging)
  • Humus content: Variable, holds nutrients well
  • Advantages: High nutrient retention
  • Disadvantages: Poor drainage, poor aeration, hard to till
  • Suitable crops: Rice, wheat (with good management)
0"LARGE"

Loamy Soil

  • Particles: Balanced mix (sand + silt + clay)
  • Structure: Crumbly, good tilth, holds shape but breaks easily
  • Porosity: Medium (balanced air spaces)
  • Capillarity: Moderate
  • Water retention: Moderate (retains enough, drains excess)
  • Humus content: Usually high
  • Advantages: Ideal fertility, good drainage & aeration
  • Disadvantages: Few (best overall soil)
  • Suitable crops: Most crops – maize, vegetables, yam, cassava

Step 2: Simple Identification & Experiments

  • Hand feel test: Gritty = sandy; Sticky = clay; Crumbly = loam
  • Jar settling test: Sand settles first, clay last
  • Funnel/percolation test: Sandy drains fastest, clay slowest
  • Capillarity test: Water rises highest in clay
  • Humus/air: Dark color = more humus; Displacement can for air

Step 3: Components of Soil (Typical Ideal Composition)

  • Inorganic (minerals/particles): \~45% – sand, silt, clay, nutrients (N, P, K, Ca)
  • Organic (humus + roots): \~5% – decayed matter, improves structure & water holding
  • Soil organisms: Bacteria, fungi, earthworms – decompose, fix nitrogen, aerate
  • Soil air: \~25% – oxygen for root respiration
  • Soil water: \~25% – dissolves & transports nutrients

Relation to Plant Growth: Balanced components → good root penetration, nutrient uptake, respiration. Excess water → root rot (poor air). Low humus/nutrients → stunted growth, deficiency symptoms. Good organisms → better nutrient cycling.

Step 4: Soil Fertility

i. Loss of Soil Fertility

  • Leaching – soluble nutrients washed away (esp. sandy soil/heavy rain)
  • Erosion – topsoil removed by wind/water
  • Compaction – pores crushed (machinery, overgrazing), poor air/water
  • Repeated monocropping – exhausts specific nutrients
  • Loss of organic/inorganic matter – reduces humus & minerals
4"LARGE"

ii. Renewal & Maintenance of Soil Fertility (Conservation Practices)

  • Crop rotation – alternates crops to restore nutrients (e.g., legumes fix N after cereals)
  • Advantages of crop rotation 🍃
  • Contour ridging – ridges along contours on slopes to trap water & reduce erosion
  • Terracing – steps on steep slopes to prevent runoff & erosion
  • Mulching – cover soil with leaves/straw (or plastic) to retain moisture, add humus, suppress weeds
  • Poly-cropping / Strip-cropping – multiple crops or strips to protect soil & use nutrients better
  • Organic fertilizers – manure, compost → adds humus & slow-release nutrients
  • Inorganic fertilizers – NPK → quick nutrient supply
  • Shifting cultivation – farm then allow fallow for natural recovery

Exam Key Points

  • Link: Soil type → characteristics (structure, porosity, capillarity, humus) → components → fertility loss → conservation methods → healthy plant growth
  • Loamy = best soil overall
  • Memorize examples, simple experiments, and conservation practices
