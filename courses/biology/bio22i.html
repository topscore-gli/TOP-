BIOLOGY

🧫 Chapter 22: Biotechnology (JAMB)

Definition + applications in agriculture, medicine, pharmaceuticals, and food industry

1️⃣ What is Biotechnology?

Definition: Biotechnology is the use of living organisms (or their parts like cells, enzymes, or genes), biological processes, or biological systems to create useful products and technologies that improve human life.

Biotechnology combines biology + technology to produce useful goods and services

JAMB definition (simple and correct): Biotechnology is the application of scientific and engineering principles to the processing of materials by biological agents to provide goods and services.

Modern biotechnology involves

  • Genetic engineering (changing DNA)
  • Recombinant DNA technology
  • Fermentation (using microbes)
  • Tissue culture / micropropagation
  • Biological enzymes (industrial use)
🧠 Memory line:

"Bio + Tech = using life to make useful things."

2️⃣ Applications of Biotechnology

JAMB focuses on 4 main areas: Agriculture, Medicine, Pharmaceuticals, Food Industry.

Genetic engineering is a major part of modern biotechnology

(i) Agriculture

Goal: Improve crops/livestock, increase yield, reduce pests, and support food security.

GM crops (e.g., Bt cotton/maize) can resist insect pests

Key applications (memorize 4–6)

  • Genetically modified crops (GMOs): Bt maize/cotton (insect-resistant), herbicide-tolerant crops
  • Pest/disease resistance: plants produce Bt toxin (natural pesticide)
  • Higher yields/faster growth: bigger/faster crops, less fertilizer needed
  • Drought/salt tolerant crops: useful in dry or salty soils (e.g., northern Nigeria conditions)
  • Biofertilizers: microbes like Rhizobium fix nitrogen → reduce chemical fertilizers
  • Improved nutrition: Golden Rice (vitamin A)
  • Tissue culture: rapid multiplication of disease-free banana, yam, cassava

⚠️ GMO Notes (JAMB)

  • Advantages: more food, less pesticide, better nutrition
  • Concerns: biosafety, environmental impact, unknown long-term effects

(ii) Medicine

Goal: Produce drugs, vaccines, tests, and treatments to fight diseases.

Recombinant DNA helps bacteria produce human insulin

Key applications (classic JAMB examples)

  • Insulin: for diabetes (produced by engineered bacteria/yeast)
  • Human growth hormone: for growth disorders
  • Interferon: antiviral protein (some cancers/hepatitis)
  • Vaccines: e.g., Hepatitis B (recombinant)
  • Monoclonal antibodies: cancer treatment/diagnosis
  • Gene therapy: correcting faulty genes (developing)
  • Diagnostics: PCR tests, ELISA
  • Antibiotics: improved production from microbes
🧠 Big Point:

Recombinant DNA allows mass production of pure medicines like insulin and vaccines.

(iii) Pharmaceuticals

Goal: Large-scale production of drugs and biological products (biologics).

Biotech produces monoclonal antibodies used in diagnosis and treatment

Key applications

  • Therapeutic proteins/enzymes (e.g., clotting factors for hemophilia)
  • Antibiotics (e.g., improved penicillin production)
  • Vaccines and hormones
  • Drug discovery (faster screening)
  • Personalized medicine (based on genetics)

(iv) Food Industry

Goal: Improve food production, processing, preservation, and safety.

Fermentation uses microbes to make foods like bread, yogurt, and drinks

Key applications

  • Fermentation: yogurt, cheese, bread, beer, wine
  • Food enzymes: amylase (brewing), rennet (cheese), pectinase (juice clarification)
  • Single-cell protein: microbes grown as protein food (yeast/algae)
  • Improved quality: longer shelf life, better taste/texture
  • Food safety: fast detection of contaminants
  • GM foods: nutrient-enhanced/long-lasting fruits and vegetables

Cheese making uses microbes and enzymes (biotechnology in food)
