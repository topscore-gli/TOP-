Energy flow + nutrient cycles + symbiotic interactions (JAMB-ready)

⚡ Part A: Energy Flow in Ecosystems

Definition (Energy flow): The movement of energy through living organisms in an ecosystem, usually from the sun → producers → consumers. Energy flows in one direction.

Energy moves upward through trophic levels (and decreases at each step)

1. 🔗 Food Chains

Definition: A food chain is a linear sequence showing how energy and nutrients pass from one organism to another.

Structure (Remember)

  • P → C → SC → TC (Producer → Consumer → Secondary → Tertiary)
  • "Plants Provide, Consumers Consume"

Simple food chain example (producer → consumers)

Level Name Description Example
1° Producers (Autotrophs) Make food by photosynthesis Grass, algae, phytoplankton
2° Primary consumers Eat producers (herbivores) Grasshopper, rabbit, zebrafish
3° Secondary consumers Eat herbivores Frog, small fish, spider
4° Tertiary consumers Top carnivores Snake, hawk, shark
— Decomposers Break down dead matter Bacteria, fungi

Example food chain: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Hawk

🔑 Key Points for JAMB

  • Energy flows one way (not recycled)
  • About 10% transfers; 90% lost as heat/respiration
  • Usually 3–5 trophic levels maximum

2. 🕸️ Food Webs

Definition: A food web is an interconnected network of multiple food chains showing complex feeding relationships.

Food webs show multiple feeding links (more stable than a single chain)

Why Food Webs Matter

  • Animals often eat more than one food type
  • Alternative food sources improve stability
  • Shows biodiversity and interdependence

3. 📊 Trophic Levels & Energy Pyramid

Definition (Trophic level): The feeding position an organism occupies in a food chain/web.

Energy pyramid: producers have the most energy

10% rule example: If producers have 10,000 kJ → primary 1,000 kJ → secondary 100 kJ → tertiary 10 kJ.

🧠 MEMORY AID:

"Energy is expensive — 90% tax at each level!"

♻️ Part B: Nutrient Cycling in Nature

Definition (Nutrient cycling): The continuous movement and recycling of nutrients (like carbon, water, nitrogen) between living organisms and the environment.

Big Idea

  • Energy flows one way
  • Nutrients are recycled

i. 🌫️ Carbon Cycle

Definition: The carbon cycle is the movement of carbon between the atmosphere, organisms, soil, and oceans.

Carbon cycle: photosynthesis removes CO₂, respiration/combustion adds CO₂

Process What Happens Direction
Photosynthesis Plants take in CO₂ Atmosphere → living things
Respiration Organisms release CO₂ Living things → atmosphere
Combustion Burning releases CO₂ Fossil fuels → atmosphere
Decomposition Decomposers release CO₂ Dead matter → atmosphere
Feeding Carbon moves up food chains Plants → animals

🌍 Carbon Cycle & Global Warming (JAMB Hot Topic)

  • Deforestation → less CO₂ removed
  • Fossil fuel burning → more CO₂ added
  • Result: greenhouse effect → global warming

ii. 💧 Water Cycle (Hydrological Cycle)

Definition: The water cycle is the continuous movement of water between the earth’s surface and the atmosphere.

Water cycle: evaporation → condensation → precipitation

Term Definition Example
Evaporation Liquid → vapor (heat driven) Oceans, lakes
Transpiration Water loss from plant leaves Trees
Condensation Vapor → droplets Cloud formation
Precipitation Water falls to earth Rain, snow
Infiltration Water soaks into ground Groundwater
Runoff Water flows over land Rivers/streams

🧠 MEMORY TIP:

"Water is the delivery truck for nutrients!"

iii. 🦠 Nitrogen Cycle (Most Complex)

Definition: The nitrogen cycle is the recycling of nitrogen through atmosphere, soil, and organisms so that plants can obtain usable nitrogen for proteins/DNA.

Nitrogen cycle: fixation → nitrification → assimilation → denitrification

Process Conversion Agent Where
Nitrogen fixation N₂ → NH₃ / nitrates Fixing bacteria Soil, root nodules
Nitrification NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ Nitrifying bacteria Soil
Assimilation NO₃⁻ → plant proteins Plants Plant tissues
Ammonification Organic N → NH₃ Decomposers Soil
Denitrification NO₃⁻ → N₂ gas Denitrifying bacteria Waterlogged soil

Examples of Nitrogen Fixers

  • Free-living: Azotobacter, Clostridium
  • Symbiotic: Rhizobium (legume root nodules)

🌱 Leguminous Plants & Rhizobium (Mutualism)

Definition (Symbiosis): A close, long-term interaction between different species living together.

Legume root nodules contain Rhizobium bacteria

Mutualism: Plant provides carbohydrates + shelter; Rhizobium fixes N₂ → ammonia/nitrates for plant.

Result: better protein growth + improved soil fertility.

Agricultural PracticeBenefit
Crop rotation with legumesReplenishes soil nitrogen naturally
Green manureAdds nutrients when ploughed into soil
Reduced fertilizer needSaves cost; less pollution
IntercroppingLegumes + cereals improve yield balance

🤝 Symbiotic Interactions Summary

Definition (Symbiotic interaction): Any close relationship between two different species living together that affects at least one of them.

Mutualism example: bee and flower

Interaction Species A Species B Example
Mutualism Benefits Benefits Bee & flower; Rhizobium & legume
Commensalism Benefits Unaffected Remora & shark; epiphyte on tree
Parasitism Benefits Harmed Tapeworm & human; dodder & plant
Saprophytism Feeds on dead matter Dead matter Fungi on bread; bacteria on carcass
Amensalism Harmed Unaffected Penicillium & bacteria
Competition Harmed Harmed Lions & hyenas for prey
Predation Benefits Killed/eaten Hawk & mouse; lion & zebra
Cooperation Benefits Benefits (not essential) Wolves in pack; bees in hive
