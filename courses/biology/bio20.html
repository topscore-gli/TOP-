1. Definition of Variation

Variation is the occurrence of differences among individuals of the same species due to genetic and/or environmental factors. It provides the raw material for natural selection and ensures adaptability and survival of populations.

2. Definition of Agglutination in Blood

Agglutination is the clumping together of red blood cells when incompatible blood types are mixed. It occurs when antigens on the surface of red blood cells react with specific antibodies in the plasma, causing the cells to stick together (e.g., during transfusion of mismatched blood).

3. Types of Variation

Continuous Variation

Gradual range with intermediates; influenced by many genes (polygenic) + environment.

  • Examples: Height, weight, skin color shades, intelligence, leaf size
  • Graph: Bell-shaped (normal distribution) curve
  • Causes: Multiple genes + environmental factors (additive effects)

Discontinuous Variation

Distinct categories with no intermediates; usually one/few genes (monogenic), minimal environmental influence.

  • Examples: Blood groups (A, B, AB, O), tongue rolling (yes/no), earlobe attachment

4. Morphological Variations (Visible/Physical)

  • Size (height/weight): Continuous – class measurement → frequency histogram (bell curve)
  • Colour (skin, eye, hair): Mostly continuous (polygenic + melanin); some discontinuous (albino/normal)
  • Fingerprints: Discontinuous – arch (\~5%), loop (\~60%), whorl (\~35%). Unique even in twins; used in forensics
  • Experiment (JAMB practical): Classify fingerprints, calculate % occurrence in population

5. Physiological Variations (Internal/Functional)

  • Tongue Rolling: Discontinuous – dominant (roller \~70%), recessive (non-roller \~30%)
  • PTC Tasting: Discontinuous – dominant (taster \~75%, bitter), recessive (non-taster)
  • Blood Groups: Discontinuous – ABO + Rh = 8 types (A±, B±, AB±, O±)

6. Causes of Variation

  • Genetic: Mutations, crossing over, independent assortment, fertilization
  • Environmental: Nutrition, climate, diseases (stronger effect on continuous traits)

JAMB Objective: Relate variation to genetics, environment, and habitat.

8. Rh Factor

Alleles: R (Rh+ dominant), r (Rh- recessive)

GenotypePhenotype
RR or RrRh+
rrRh-

Example cross: Rr × Rr → 75% Rh+, 25% Rh-

Rh Incompatibility: Rh- mother + Rh+ fetus → antibodies attack later Rh+ fetuses (hemolytic disease: jaundice, anemia). Prevented by RhoGAM.

9. Combined ABO + Rh Inheritance

8 blood types (A+, A-, B+, B-, AB+, AB-, O+, O-). Inherited independently (different chromosomes).

Example: A+ (I^A i Rr) × B+ (I^B i Rr) → all 8 types possible.

10. Applications of Discontinuous Variation

  • Crime Detection: Fingerprints (unique), blood groups/DNA narrow suspects
  • Blood Transfusion: Match ABO + Rh to avoid agglutination
  • Paternity Determination: Rule out impossible matches (e.g., O parents cannot have AB child)

Quick Exam Mantra (JAMB 2026)

  • Continuous → polygenic + environment → range + bell curve
  • Discontinuous → monogenic → distinct categories → Mendelian inheritance
  • Applications → fingerprints (crime), blood groups (transfusion/paternity)
