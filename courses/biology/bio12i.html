🧠 A. Nervous Coordination

Definition: The process by which the nervous system detects, processes, and responds to stimuli through electrical signals (nerve impulses) to coordinate body activities.

Nervous system overview: CNS + PNS

i. Central Nervous System (CNS)

CNS = Brain + Spinal cord

Component Structure Function
Brain Protected by skull; 3 main parts (cerebrum, cerebellum, medulla) Control center for body activities
Spinal Cord Protected by vertebrae; runs from brain down the back Connects brain to body; reflex center

← Swipe table to see more →

🧠 Brain Parts Mnemonic: "CCM"

Cerebrum = thinking/memory/voluntary movement | Cerebellum = balance/coordination | Medulla = breathing/heartbeat/swallowing

🔑 JAMB Tip

  • The medulla controls involuntary actions you can’t stop (breathing, heartbeat).

ii. Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

Definition: The network of nerves that connects the central nervous system to all other parts of the body, carrying signals to and from the brain and spinal cord.

PNS: cranial nerves + spinal nerves (outside CNS)

Component Function
Cranial nerves (12 pairs) Connect brain to head/neck organs
Spinal nerves (31 pairs) Connect spinal cord to the rest of the body
Autonomic system Controls internal organs automatically

← Swipe table to see more →

Quick line: PNS = everything outside brain & spinal cord.

iii. Mechanism of Impulse Transmission

Definition: The process by which electrical signals (action potentials) travel along neurons and cross synapses using chemical messengers to communicate information throughout the body.

Pathway:

STIMULUS → RECEPTOR → SENSORY NEURON → CNS → MOTOR NEURON → EFFECTOR → RESPONSE

Neuron: dendrites → cell body → axon → synapse

🧩 Neuron Structure (Functions)

  • Dendrites → receive signals
  • Cell body → processes information
  • Axon → transmits signals away
  • Myelin sheath → insulates & speeds transmission
  • Synapse → gap where neurotransmitters cross

Synapse: neurotransmitters carry signal across the gap

🧠 ONE-WAY STREET:

Dendrite → Cell body → Axon (signals don’t travel backwards in normal flow)

iv. Reflex Action

Definition: An automatic, rapid, and involuntary response to a stimulus that occurs without conscious thought, mediated mainly by the spinal cord to protect the body from harm.

Reflex arc: spinal cord coordinates the quick response

Reflex Arc Pathway:

Stimulus → Receptor → Sensory neuron → Spinal cord → Motor neuron → Effector → Response

Reflex Stimulus Response
Knee jerk Tap below kneecap Leg kicks out
Blinking Object near eye Eyelids close
Pupil reflex Bright light Pupil constricts
Withdrawal Touch hot object Hand pulls away

⚠️ Why Reflexes Are Fast

  • They skip the brain at first (spinal cord handles it directly).

Sense organs contain receptors that detect stimuli

iv. The Eye (Detailed)

Eye structure: cornea, lens, retina, optic nerve

👁️ Key Structures (What They Do)

  • Cornea → refracts light
  • Iris → controls pupil size
  • Pupil → hole that lets light in
  • Lens → focuses image (accommodation)
  • Retina → rods (dim) & cones (color/bright)
  • Optic nerve → carries impulses to brain

How Vision Works: Light enters → lens focuses on retina → rods/cones convert to impulses → optic nerve → brain interprets image.

Common Eye Defects

Myopia vs Hyperopia (and astigmatism) — how images focus

Defect Cause Effect Correction
Myopia (short-sighted) Eyeball too long Near clear, far blurry Concave lens
Hyperopia (long-sighted) Eyeball too short Far clear, near blurry Convex lens
Astigmatism Uneven cornea curvature Blur/distortion Cylindrical lens
Cataract Cloudy lens Reduced vision Surgery (lens replacement)

🧠 Eye Defects:

Myopia = “My eyes see near” | Hyperopia = “Hyper eyes see far”

v. The Ear (Detailed)

Ear: outer, middle, inner — hearing + balance

Part Contains Function
Outer ear Pinna, ear canal Collects & directs sound
Middle ear Eardrum, ossicles (hammer, anvil, stirrup) Amplifies vibrations
Inner ear Cochlea, semicircular canals Hearing (cochlea) & balance (canals)

How Hearing Works: eardrum vibrates → ossicles amplify → oval window → cochlea fluid moves → hair cells → auditory nerve → brain.

Balance: semicircular canals detect head movement/position.

🧪 C. Hormonal Control

Definition: Regulation of body activities through chemical messengers (hormones) produced by endocrine glands and transported in blood to target organs.

Major endocrine glands (top-to-bottom)

🧠 Glands Mnemonic:

"PATPAG" — Pituitary, Adrenal, Thyroid, Parathyroid, Pancreas, (And) Gonads

i. Animal Endocrine System

Gland Location Hormone(s) Function
Pituitary (Master) Base of brain GH, TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, ADH, Oxytocin Controls other glands; growth; water balance; childbirth
Thyroid Neck (front of trachea) Thyroxine Metabolism rate; growth/development
Parathyroid Behind thyroid PTH Calcium/phosphate balance
Adrenal Above kidneys Adrenaline Fight/flight response
Pancreas Behind stomach Insulin & Glucagon Blood sugar regulation
Gonads Pelvic area Estrogen/Progesterone, Testosterone Sexual development + reproduction

← Swipe table to see more →

🔑 Must-Remember Notes

  • Pituitary = master gland (controls others)
  • Adrenaline increases heart rate + breathing + glucose release
  • Insulin lowers blood sugar; Glucagon raises blood sugar

ii. Plant Hormones (Phytohormones)

Definition: Chemicals produced in plants that regulate growth, development, and responses at very low concentrations.

Phototropism: auxin causes bending toward light

Hormone Main Functions Applications
Auxins (IAA) Cell elongation; root/shoot growth; phototropism & geotropism Weed killers (2,4-D); rooting powder
Gibberellins Stem elongation; seed germination; fruit development Seedless grapes; bigger fruits
Cytokinins Cell division; delay leaf aging; lateral bud growth Tissue culture; keep cut flowers fresh
Ethylene Fruit ripening; leaf abscission Artificial ripening of bananas/tomatoes
ABA Inhibits growth; stomatal closure; seed dormancy Drought stress responses

Tropisms (Plant Movements)

  • Phototropism → toward light
  • Geotropism → response to gravity (roots +, shoots -)
  • Hydrotropism → toward water
  • Thigmotropism → response to touch (tendrils)

🏠 D. Homeostasis

Definition: Maintenance of a constant internal environment (temperature, pH, water, salts, glucose) within narrow limits for proper enzyme/cell function.

Homeostasis keeps internal conditions stable

i. Body Temperature Regulation

Normal body temperature: 37°C (98.6°F)

Hypothalamus: body’s thermostat (control center)

When Too HOT When Too COLD
Sweating (evaporation cools skin) Shivering (muscle contraction generates heat)
Vasodilation (blood vessels widen) Vasoconstriction (blood vessels narrow)
Hair lies flat (less insulation) Hair stands (goosebumps trap air)
Less thyroxine produced More thyroxine (increases metabolism)

🧠 Temperature:

"SHIVER when cold, SWEAT when hot"

ii. Salt & Water Regulation (Osmoregulation)

Definition: Regulation of water and salts in body fluids to maintain osmotic balance and proper cell function.

Kidney nephron: key unit for water/salt regulation

Organs Involved

  • Kidneys
  • Skin
  • Lungs
  • Liver

ADH (from pituitary) controls water reabsorption:

  • Hot day / dehydrated: more ADH → more water reabsorbed → concentrated urine (small volume)
  • Cold day / hydrated: less ADH → less water reabsorbed → dilute urine (large volume)

Aldosterone (adrenal cortex): controls sodium/potassium balance.
