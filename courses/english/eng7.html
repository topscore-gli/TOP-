1️⃣ Mechanics

Mechanics means the rules of correct written English: spelling, punctuation, and capitalisation.

Where to use mechanics correctly

Main rules

  • Use British spelling: colour, realise, programme, defence
  • Use apostrophe correctly: boy’s, boys’, don’t
  • Use direct speech punctuation correctly
  • Capitalise I, proper nouns, days, months, and official titles when specific

2️⃣ Ordinary Usage

Ordinary usage means the literal meaning of a word or expression.

Where to use ordinary meaning correctly

Ask yourself: What does the word mean exactly as written, without metaphor or idiom?

The bank of the river was slippery.
Meaning: Side or edge of the river
Why: This is literal geographical meaning.
The light in the room was too dim to read.
Meaning: Brightness or illumination
He ran to catch the bus.
Meaning: Moved quickly on foot
The head of the school addressed the students.
Meaning: The principal or leader
She bought a new pair of shoes.
Meaning: Two matching items
The cold weather made him shiver.
Meaning: Low temperature
The bark of the dog woke me up.
Meaning: The sound a dog makes
He drew a picture of his house.
Meaning: Made a sketch
The key to the door was missing.
Meaning: A metal device used to open a lock
She planted flowers in the garden.
Meaning: Put plants into the soil

3️⃣ Figurative Usage

Figurative usage is non-literal language. JAMB may ask for the figure of speech, the intended meaning, or the best explanation.

Where figurative usage is used

Main clue

  • If the sentence is not true literally, it is likely figurative
  • Look for comparison, exaggeration, human qualities, contradiction, or sound imitation

Best strategy

  • Separate literal meaning from figurative meaning
  • Memorise common figures of speech by type
  • Memorise idioms exactly — never guess from the individual words
  • Revise in daily batches and test yourself often
