📘 Word Classes + Concord Mastery Guide

JAMB 2026 • all 8 parts of speech • rules • positions • usage • concord for top score

📋 Quick Overview

Word classes, also called parts of speech, are the foundation of English grammar. JAMB tests them in Lexis & Structure, concord, phrasal verbs, idioms, and comprehension.

  • There are 8 main word classes
  • JAMB tests their definitions, positions, rules, and correct usage
  • Concord is strongly linked to nouns, pronouns, and verbs
🧠 JAMB Goal

Know what each class does, where it appears in a sentence, and the rule that controls it.

1️⃣ Nouns

What it is: A noun names a person, place, thing, animal, idea, or quality.

Where nouns are used

  • As the subject of a sentence
  • As the object of a verb
  • After prepositions
  • As complement after linking verbs

Common JAMB nouns

  • committee, family, police, luggage, furniture, information, news, mathematics
  • equipment, advice, baggage, stationery, poultry, cattle, people
  • scissors, trousers, glasses, economics, physics, measles, athletics
  • headquarters, government, audience, crowd, team, jury, staff, public
  • majority, minority, youth, offspring, aircraft, deer, sheep, series, species

Rules for JAMB

Important noun rules

  • Collective nouns usually take singular verbs: The committee is…
  • Uncountable nouns do not take “a/an” and usually do not take plural “s”
  • Always-plural nouns take plural verbs: police have, scissors are, trousers are
  • Some nouns look plural but are singular in meaning: mathematics is, economics is
The committee is meeting next week.
Explanation: “Committee” is a collective noun, treated as one unit, so singular verb “is”.
All the information you need is on the website.
Explanation: “Information” is uncountable. Use singular verb “is”, never “informations”.
The police have arrested the suspect.
Explanation: “Police” is always plural, so use “have”.
His luggage was lost at the airport.
Explanation: “Luggage” is uncountable, so singular verb “was”.
Mathematics is my favourite subject.
Explanation: “Mathematics” is singular in this sense.

2️⃣ Verbs

What it is: A verb shows action, state, or occurrence.

Where verbs are used

  • To show what the subject does
  • To show the subject’s state or condition
  • In phrasal verbs, where meaning changes with a particle

Common JAMB verbs and phrasal verbs

  • prefer, resemble, deliver, circumvent, coax, coerce
  • cash in on, run on, postpone, break down, give up
  • look forward to, turn down, put up with, come across, call off
  • break into, look into, take after, bring about, put off
  • make up, fall out, get along, run out of, look up to, turn up

Rules for JAMB

Important verb rules

  • Choose verbs that fit the meaning, not just the tense
  • Phrasal verbs often have meanings different from the main verb alone
  • Some verbs take special prepositions: look forward to, put up with, run on petrol
  • Some formal expressions are common in JAMB: delivered of a baby
The thief tried to circumvent the security system.
Explanation: “Circumvent” means get round or avoid.
Ayayi cashed in on our defensive error to score.
Explanation: “Cash in on” means take advantage of.
The car runs on petrol, not diesel.
Explanation: “Run on” shows fuel source.
She was delivered of a bouncing baby boy yesterday.
Explanation: Formal JAMB usage: “delivered of” means gave birth to.
The match was called off because of rain.
Explanation: “Call off” means cancel.

3️⃣ Adjectives

What it is: An adjective describes or modifies a noun or pronoun.

Where adjectives are used

  • Before a noun: a beautiful dress
  • After a linking verb: the dress is beautiful

Common JAMB adjectives

  • beautiful, repulsive, lovely, white, best, little, few
  • affluent, benevolent, candid, frugal, elated, adamant, hostile
  • lucid, meticulous, sluggish, mischievous, phenomenal, bureaucratic
  • eccentric, exhilarating, unprecedented, unanimous, irresistible
  • immaculate, excruciating, inquisitive, unpredictable, sophisticated, obsolete

Rules for JAMB

Important adjective rules

  • Adjectives modify nouns and pronouns, not verbs
  • After linking verbs, use adjective, not adverb: She feels bad, not badly
  • Comparative forms must be correct: more expensive, better, less interesting
  • Some words can confuse students: little/few, less/fewer
She bought a beautiful dress for the party.
Explanation: “Beautiful” comes before the noun “dress”.
Everybody hates him for his repulsive looks.
Explanation: “Repulsive” modifies “looks”.
It is more expensive to travel on Friday.
Explanation: Comparative adjective used after linking verb “is”.
That was a lovely evening.
Explanation: “Lovely” modifies “evening”.
Jasmines are white in colour.
Explanation: “White” comes after linking verb “are”.

4️⃣ Adverbs

What it is: An adverb modifies a verb, an adjective, or another adverb.

Where adverbs are used

  • To show how: quickly, beautifully
  • To show when: later, soon, yesterday
  • To show where: here, there, upstairs
  • To show degree: very, quite, almost, too

Common JAMB adverbs

  • beautifully, pleasantly, quickly, later, early, more
  • slowly, very, quite, rather, too, extremely
  • frequently, seldom, always, never, soon, yesterday
  • here, there, everywhere, upstairs, downstairs
  • well, badly, hard, fast, almost, nearly, hardly, carefully, suddenly

Rules for JAMB

Important adverb rules

  • Adverbs usually modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs
  • Do not confuse adjective and adverb forms: quick/quickly, slow/slowly
  • Some adverbs have the same form as adjectives: fast, hard, late, early
  • “Hardly” does not mean “hard”; it means “scarcely”
She sang beautifully on stage.
Explanation: “Beautifully” modifies the verb “sang”.
I was pleasantly surprised when she arrived.
Explanation: “Pleasantly” modifies adjective “surprised”.
John came later than Peter.
Explanation: Comparative adverb of time.
If we finish our work quickly, we can go to the movies.
Explanation: “Quickly” modifies “finish”.
He runs more quickly than his brother.
Explanation: Comparative adverb form.

5️⃣ Pronouns

What it is: A pronoun replaces a noun to avoid repetition.

Where pronouns are used

  • As subject: he, she, they
  • As object: him, her, them
  • As possessive: mine, yours, theirs
  • As relative pronouns: who, whom, which, that

Common JAMB pronouns

  • you, theirs, it, he, she, they, we, I, me, him, her, us, them
  • mine, yours, his, hers, its, ours, whose, who, whom, which, that
  • everyone, somebody, anybody, nobody, something, anything
  • this, that, these, those, one, each, either, neither

Rules for JAMB

Important pronoun rules

  • Pronoun must agree with the noun it replaces
  • Use subject pronoun as subject, object pronoun as object
  • Indefinite pronouns like everyone, somebody, each are usually singular
  • Possessive pronouns stand alone: mine, yours, theirs
It happened that our dog is male but theirs are all females.
Explanation: “Theirs” replaces “their dogs”.
Where are you going?
Explanation: “You” is a second person pronoun.
Everyone has his own book.
Explanation: “Everyone” is treated as singular in JAMB concord.
The book is mine, not yours.
Explanation: Possessive pronouns stand without a noun after them.
Neither the teacher nor the students are ready.
Explanation: With neither…nor, the verb agrees with the nearer subject “students”.

6️⃣ Prepositions

What it is: A preposition shows the relationship of a noun or pronoun to other words in a sentence.

Where prepositions are used

  • To show place: under, above, beside
  • To show time: before, during, since, until
  • To show direction: into, toward, across
  • In fixed expressions: in spite of, on the radio, on petrol

Common JAMB prepositions

  • under, for, on, in spite of, beneath, with, by, from, at, in, to, of
  • about, above, across, against, along, among, around, before, behind
  • below, beside, between, beyond, during, except, inside, into, near
  • off, onto, outside, over, since, through, throughout, toward, until, upon

Rules for JAMB

Important preposition rules

  • Some verbs and expressions take fixed prepositions
  • Use the correct collocation: on the radio, on television, on petrol
  • Do not confuse similar prepositions: between/among, in/into, on/upon
  • Prepositions are often tested in idioms and phrasal expressions
I left my shoes under the kitchen table.
Explanation: “Under” shows lower position.
We heard the news on the radio.
Explanation: Correct JAMB collocation: on the radio.
The car runs on petrol, not diesel.
Explanation: “On” is used for fuel source.
We went out in spite of the rain.
Explanation: “In spite of” means despite.
He felt it was beneath his dignity to join the queue.
Explanation: “Beneath” is used in formal expression.

7️⃣ Conjunctions

What it is: A conjunction joins words, phrases, or clauses.

Where conjunctions are used

  • To join words: tea and coffee
  • To join phrases: in the room or outside
  • To join clauses: he came because he was invited

Common JAMB conjunctions

  • but, although, rather than, because, since, for, and, or, yet, so
  • if, unless, while, whereas, even though, as, when, whenever
  • where, wherever, before, after, until, till, as soon as
  • provided that, in order that, so that

Rules for JAMB

Important conjunction rules

  • Coordinating conjunctions join equal structures
  • Subordinating conjunctions introduce dependent clauses
  • Be careful with contrast markers: although, though, but, yet
  • Some phrases function like conjunctions: rather than, in order that, so that
Although the problem was simple, few students solved it.
Explanation: “Although” introduces contrast and a dependent clause.
I prefer tea rather than coffee.
Explanation: “Rather than” expresses preference.
We went out although it was raining.
Explanation: Contrast conjunction.
He ran fast because he was late.
Explanation: “Because” introduces reason.
Since you are here, let us start the meeting.
Explanation: “Since” can show reason or time, depending on context.

8️⃣ Interjections

What it is: An interjection expresses sudden feeling or emotion.

Where interjections are used

  • At the beginning of a sentence
  • Before an exclamation mark or comma
  • To show pain, surprise, joy, regret, relief, praise

Common JAMB interjections

  • Ouch, Wow, Ah, Oh, Hey, Alas, Hurray, Bravo, Oops, Aha
  • Eh, Well, Gosh, Gee, Yippee, Hurrah, Phew, Ugh, Yay, Boo

Rules for JAMB

Important interjection rules

  • Interjections are not grammatically tied to the rest of the sentence
  • They are usually followed by an exclamation mark
  • Meaning depends on emotion expressed
Ouch! That hurts.
Explanation: “Ouch” expresses pain.
Wow! That is amazing.
Explanation: “Wow” expresses surprise or admiration.
Alas! The team lost the match.
Explanation: “Alas” expresses sorrow or regret.
Bravo! You have done well.
Explanation: “Bravo” expresses praise.
Phew! I finally finished the exam.
Explanation: “Phew” expresses relief.

9️⃣ Rules of Concord (Subject-Verb Agreement) – JAMB Focus

Concord means the subject and verb must agree in number and sometimes person. JAMB loves tricky concord questions.

15 key concord rules

  • 1. Singular subject takes singular verb; plural subject takes plural verb
  • 2. Collective nouns are usually singular
  • 3. With as well as / with / together with / along with, ignore the extra words
  • 4. In either…or / neither…nor, agree with the nearer subject
  • 5. A number of = plural; The number of = singular
  • 6. Indefinite pronouns like everyone, somebody, each are singular
  • 7. Uncountables like news, mathematics take singular verbs; police, cattle take plural verbs
  • 8. More than one takes singular verb
  • 9. The + adjective (the poor, the rich) is plural
  • 10. Amounts of money, time, distance often take singular verbs
  • 11. With not / not only…but also, focus on the true subject before the interruption
  • 12. Relative pronouns agree with their antecedents
  • 13. One of / each of usually takes singular verb
  • 14. None of is often treated as singular in JAMB
  • 15. Subjects joined by and are plural, unless they express one idea
The teacher, as well as his students, is working very hard.
Rule: Ignore “as well as his students”; main subject is “teacher”.
Either my friends or my brother has taken the car out.
Rule: Verb agrees with nearer subject “brother”.
A number of students are absent today.
Rule: “A number of” is plural.
The number of women is more than that of men.
Rule: “The number of” is singular.
More than one student has failed.
Rule: “More than one” takes singular verb.
The poor are always with us.
Rule: “The + adjective” is plural.
Twenty thousand naira is too much for the book.
Rule: Amount of money is treated as singular.
My wife and comforter has arrived.
Rule: One person is being described in two ways, so singular verb.
