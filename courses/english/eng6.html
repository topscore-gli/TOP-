1️⃣ Mood

Mood shows the speaker’s attitude.

Main types and where to use them

  • Indicative → for facts and real questions
  • Imperative → for commands and instructions
  • Subjunctive → most tested in JAMB

Where to use the subjunctive correctly

Use base verb form after:

  • suggest that
  • demand that
  • recommend that
  • insist that
  • essential that
  • important that
  • necessary that
  • would rather
  • it is time / high time
  • as if / as though in unreal situations
  • if I were you / wish + unreal situation

Main rule: In unreal or demanded situations, use the base form of the verb. Also use were for all subjects in unreal conditions.

The Principal suggested that the unruly student be expelled immediately.
Why: After “suggested that”, use subjunctive base form → be.
Maryam treats people as if she were better than they are.
Why: “As if” + unreal idea → use were.
It is high time we left the meeting.
Why: “High time” takes past subjunctive form.
If I were you, I would forgive the boy.
Why: Unreal condition → use were, not was.
The teacher demanded that every student be present.
Why: After “demanded that”, use base form.
She wishes she were rich.
Why: Wish + unreal condition → were.

2️⃣ Tense

Tense shows the time of an action.

Where to use each tense correctly

Main tense clues

  • Present Simple → habits, routines, general truths
  • Past Simple → completed past actions, often with yesterday, last week
  • Future → actions expected later
  • Past Perfect → one past action happened before another past action
  • Present Perfect → action from past connected to now
  • Future Perfect → action completed before a future time

JAMB key: always match tense with time signals like yesterday, since, by next year, when, before.

I had hardly lain down to sleep when I heard the gunshot.
Why: “Had hardly” needs past participle. Lie (recline) → lay → lain.
By 11:00 a.m., I will have been writing the test for two hours.
Why: Future time + duration → future perfect continuous.
When I entered the room, they were eating dinner.
Why: Ongoing action interrupted by a past action → past continuous.
She goes to school every day.
Why: Habitual action → present simple.
Yesterday, he went to the market.
Why: “Yesterday” signals past simple.
They had finished the work before the teacher arrived.
Why: Earlier past action before another past action → past perfect.
By next year, I will have been in this school for five years.
Why: Future point + duration → future perfect.
We haven’t eaten anything since morning.
Why: “Since” + action up to now → present perfect.

3️⃣ Aspect

Aspect shows how the action is viewed: simple, continuous, perfect, or perfect continuous.

Where to use each aspect correctly

  • Simple → ordinary fact or action
  • Continuous → action in progress
  • Perfect → completed action
  • Perfect Continuous → action continuing for a period up to a point

Quick aspect rules

  • Use continuous for ongoing action
  • Use perfect for completed action with result
  • Use perfect continuous when duration matters: for two hours, since morning
The students had been studying for two hours before the break.
Why: Ongoing action up to a past point → past perfect continuous.
She has been reading the book since morning.
Why: Action began earlier and is still continuing.
By the time we arrive, they will have finished the match.
Why: Completion before future time → future perfect.
While I was sleeping, the phone rang.
Why: Interrupted ongoing action → past continuous.
They have completed the project already.
Why: Completed action with present relevance → present perfect.
He had been walking in the rain for hours when we found him.
Why: Duration continued up to past discovery point.

4️⃣ Number (Subject-Verb Concord)

Number means subject and verb must agree in singular or plural form.

Where to use singular and plural correctly

High-frequency JAMB rules

  • Ignore phrases like with, as well as, together with
  • With either…or / neither…nor, agree with the nearer subject
  • The number of = singular
  • A number of = plural
  • Collective nouns are usually singular
  • One of = singular
  • Every boy and every girl = singular
The farmer, with his sons, grows millet.
Why: Ignore “with his sons”; main subject is singular.
One of the ladies who sell in the premises has been asked to withdraw.
Why: “Ladies” → sell; “One” → has.
Either Ekaette or you are to go.
Why: Verb agrees with nearer subject “you”.
The number of accidents is increasing.
Why: “The number” is singular.
A number of students are absent today.
Why: “A number of” is plural.
Neither the teacher nor the students are happy.
Why: Nearest subject “students” is plural.
Every boy and every girl is invited.
Why: Each “every” item is treated singly.

5️⃣ Degree of Comparison

Degree shows comparison in adjectives and adverbs.

Where to use each degree correctly

  • Positive → as...as
  • Comparative → -er / more ... than
  • Superlative → -est / most ...

Main JAMB rules

  • Use comparative when comparing two things
  • Use superlative for three or more
  • Use of the two with comparative, not superlative
  • Use as...as for equal comparison
  • Short adjectives often take -er/-est; long ones take more/most
Which of the two questions is more difficult?
Why: “Of the two” requires comparative.
This is the best movie I have ever watched.
Why: Superlative fits “the ... ever”.
No other city is as large as Mumbai.
Why: Equal comparison pattern = as...as.
She is taller than her sister.
Why: Comparison between two people.
Mount Everest is the highest mountain in the world.
Why: One above all others → superlative.
Gold is more expensive than silver.
Why: Long adjective → use “more”.

6️⃣ Question Tags

Question tags are short questions added to statements for confirmation.

Where to use them correctly

Main question tag rules

  • Positive statement → negative tag
  • Negative statement → positive tag
  • Use the same auxiliary verb as in the statement
  • If there is no auxiliary, use do / does / did
  • I am → aren’t I?
  • Let’s → shall we?
You can travel on your own, can’t you?
Why: Positive statement → negative tag with same auxiliary.
He does not seem to know the answer, does he?
Why: Negative statement → positive tag.
He wrote to you, didn’t he?
Why: Past simple with no auxiliary → use “did”.
You don’t like Mathematics, do you?
Why: Negative statement → positive tag.
Students will always blame their teachers, won’t they?
Why: Future auxiliary “will” takes “won’t” in the tag.
Let’s go home, shall we?
Why: Special rule for “let’s”.
You are coming tomorrow, aren’t you?
Why: Positive “are” → negative “aren’t”.

Main rule: After have / has / had, use the past participle, not the simple past.

Special lie / lay rule

Do not mix these three

  • lie = recline → lay → lain
  • lay = put/place something → laid → laid
  • lie = tell falsehood → lied → lied
Yesterday I lay on the bed for one hour.
Meaning: reclined → past of lie.
I have lain there since noon.
Meaning: past participle of lie (recline).
She laid the book on the table.
Meaning: put or placed something.
He has gone to school.
Why: After “has”, use past participle “gone”, not “went”.

8️⃣ Final Rules for 100% JAMB Success

Best revision plan

  • Learn the trigger words for each grammar area
  • Underline time markers before choosing tense
  • Look for demand, suggestion, wish, as if, high time → think subjunctive
  • Check whether the subject is singular or plural before choosing a verb
  • Count how many things are compared before choosing degree
  • In question tags, copy the auxiliary and reverse the polarity
  • Memorize irregular verbs, especially go/went/gone and lie/lay/lain
