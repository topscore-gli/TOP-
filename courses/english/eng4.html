📋 Quick Overview of Clauses

A clause is a group of words that contains a subject and a verb. Clauses are the building blocks of sentences, and they are important in JAMB Use of English, especially in sentence completion, comprehension, and structure questions.

  • Independent Clause → can stand alone as a full sentence
  • Dependent Clause → cannot stand alone; depends on a main clause
  • Main dependent types: Noun Clause, Adjective Clause, Adverb Clause
Expanded Example 1
Sentence: The teacher praised the student who scored highest.
Independent Clause: The teacher praised the student
Dependent Clause: who scored highest
Type: Adjective clause describing “student”
Expanded Example 2
Sentence: I will go if it stops raining.
Independent Clause: I will go
Dependent Clause: if it stops raining
Type: Adverb clause of condition
Expanded Example 3
Sentence: What you said is true.
Dependent Clause: What you said
Function: Subject of the sentence
Type: Noun clause
🧠 JAMB Tip

Clauses often appear in Lexis and Structure. Practice identifying whether the clause acts as a noun, describes a noun, or modifies a verb.

1️⃣ Noun Clause – Where & How to Use

A noun clause acts like a noun. It can function as the subject, object, complement, or object of a preposition.

Common starter words

  • what, that, who, whoever, whom, whose
  • which, whether, if, why, how, where, when

Positions where noun clauses are used

  • As subject of a sentence
  • As object of a verb
  • As object of a preposition
  • As complement after a linking verb
What he said surprised me.
Noun Clause: What he said
Function: Subject of “surprised”
Step-by-step: “he” = subject, “said” = verb; the whole clause acts as one noun.
I know that she will pass.
Noun Clause: that she will pass
Function: Object of “know”
Step-by-step: It tells what I know.
The problem is whether we have enough time.
Noun Clause: whether we have enough time
Function: Subject complement
Step-by-step: It explains what the problem is.
Tell me who won the prize.
Noun Clause: who won the prize
Function: Object of “tell”
She is worried about whether it will rain.
Noun Clause: whether it will rain
Function: Object of preposition “about”
Whoever arrives first gets the seat.
Noun Clause: Whoever arrives first
Function: Subject of “gets”
I wonder why he left early.
Noun Clause: why he left early
Function: Object of “wonder”
The question is how we can solve this problem.
Noun Clause: how we can solve this problem
Function: Subject complement
She asked where the library is.
Noun Clause: where the library is
Function: Object of “asked”
🧠 JAMB Tip

If the clause can be replaced with “it” or “something” and the sentence still makes sense, it is likely a noun clause.

2️⃣ Adjective Clause (Relative Clause) – Where & How to Use

An adjective clause acts like an adjective. It describes a noun or pronoun and usually comes directly after the word it describes.

Common starter words

  • who, whom, whose, which, that
  • where, when, why

Important note

Adjective clauses may be essential (no commas) or non-essential (with commas).

The boy who helped me is my friend.
Clause: who helped me
Describes: boy
Type: Essential clause
The book which I borrowed is interesting.
Clause: which I borrowed
Describes: book
Type: Essential clause
The man whose car was stolen reported to the police.
Clause: whose car was stolen
Describes: man
This is the house where I grew up.
Clause: where I grew up
Describes: house
The day when we met was special.
Clause: when we met
Describes: day
The reason why he left is unknown.
Clause: why he left
Describes: reason
My brother, who lives in Lagos, is a doctor.
Clause: who lives in Lagos
Describes: brother
Type: Non-essential clause, so commas are used
The car that I bought last year is reliable.
Clause: that I bought last year
Describes: car
The girl whom we saw at the party is my cousin.
Clause: whom we saw at the party
Describes: girl
🧠 JAMB Tip

If the clause is necessary to identify the noun, no commas. If it only adds extra information, use commas.

3️⃣ Adverb Clause – Where & How to Use

An adverb clause acts like an adverb. It modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb. It answers questions like when, where, why, how, and under what condition.

Common starter words

  • because, since, as, if, unless
  • when, while, after, before, until
  • although, though, even though
  • so that, in order that
  • where, wherever, as if, as though

Main categories

  • Time: when, while, before, after, until
  • Place: where, wherever
  • Reason: because, since, as
  • Condition: if, unless, provided that
  • Purpose: so that, in order that
  • Concession: although, though, even though
  • Manner: as if, as though

Comma Rule: If the adverb clause comes first, use a comma. If it comes last, usually no comma.

When school ends, go home.
Clause: When school ends
Type: Time clause
Stay where you are.
Clause: where you are
Type: Place clause
He passed because he studied.
Clause: because he studied
Type: Reason clause
If you read, you’ll succeed.
Clause: If you read
Type: Condition clause
Study so that you pass.
Clause: so that you pass
Type: Purpose clause
Although tired, he continued.
Clause: Although tired
Type: Concession clause
He acts as if he owns the place.
Clause: as if he owns the place
Type: Manner clause
Unless you hurry, you’ll be late.
Clause: Unless you hurry
Type: Condition clause
She sang while she cooked.
Clause: while she cooked
Type: Time clause
🧠 JAMB Tip

Adverb clauses often come first in JAMB questions to test comma usage. Clause first = comma. Clause last = usually no comma.

4️⃣ Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS) – Where & How to Use

Coordinating conjunctions join equal parts of a sentence: words, phrases, or two independent clauses.

FANBOYS

  • For
  • And
  • Nor
  • But
  • Or
  • Yet
  • So

Comma Rule: When FANBOYS joins two independent clauses, use a comma before the conjunction.

I studied hard, and I passed.
Clause 1: I studied hard
Clause 2: I passed
Conjunction: and
She wanted to come, but she was sick.
Conjunction: but
Use: Shows contrast
Read the book, or watch the movie.
Conjunction: or
Use: Shows choice
It rained heavily, so we cancelled the match.
Conjunction: so
Use: Shows result
He is smart, yet he failed.
Conjunction: yet
Use: Shows unexpected contrast
The test was easy, for everyone prepared well.
Conjunction: for
Use: Gives reason
He doesn't smoke, nor does he drink.
Conjunction: nor
Use: Adds a second negative idea
You can eat now or later.
Use: Joins words/phrases, not two full clauses
Comma: No comma needed here
🧠 JAMB Tip

FANBOYS joins equals and makes compound sentences. Subordinating conjunctions create complex sentences.

6️⃣ Practice Tips for JAMB Success

Simple method

  • Underline the clause
  • Ask: Does it act as a noun?
  • Ask: Does it describe a noun?
  • Ask: Does it modify a verb, adjective, or adverb?
  • Check the starter word
  • Remember: FANBOYS = equal join, others often = dependent join
🧠 Final revision tip

Revise with past questions. Clauses appear every year in JAMB structure questions.

7️⃣ Mini Self-Quiz

1. In “What he said surprised me,” the clause “What he said” is a
A. adjective clause
B. noun clause
C. adverb clause
D. coordinating clause
Answer: B — noun clause.
2. In “The boy who helped me is my friend,” the clause “who helped me” is a
A. noun clause
B. adverb clause
C. adjective clause
D. independent clause
Answer: C — adjective clause.
3. In “If you read, you’ll succeed,” the clause “If you read” is an
A. adverb clause
B. noun clause
C. adjective clause
D. independent clause
Answer: A — adverb clause.
4. Which of these is a coordinating conjunction?
A. because
B. although
C. but
D. when
Answer: C — but.
5. In “My brother, who lives in Lagos, is a doctor,” the clause is
A. noun and essential
B. adjective and non-essential
C. adverb and essential
D. noun and non-essential
Answer: B — adjective and non-essential.
