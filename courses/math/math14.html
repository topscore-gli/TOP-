📋 Introduction to Coordinate Geometry

This chapter focuses on points and lines on the Cartesian plane. You will learn how to find distance, midpoint, gradient, line relationships, and equations of straight lines.

Coordinate Geometry is very formula-based, so once you know the formulas and when to use them, most questions become direct.

  • Distance comes from Pythagoras’ theorem
  • Midpoint is found by averaging coordinates
  • Gradient tells how steep a line is
  • Parallel lines have equal gradients
  • Perpendicular lines have gradients whose product is -1
🧠 Big idea

Most coordinate geometry problems are just geometry plus algebra. Sketch the points, then apply the correct formula carefully.

📊 Complete Formula Summary

Concept Formula Variables
Distance d = √[(x₂-x₁)²+(y₂-y₁)²] Two points (x₁,y₁), (x₂,y₂)
Midpoint M = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2) Average of coordinates
Gradient m = (y₂-y₁)/(x₂-x₁) Change in y over change in x
Parallel lines m₁ = m₂ Same gradient
Perpendicular lines m₁ × m₂ = -1 Negative reciprocals
Slope-intercept form y = mx + c m = gradient, c = y-intercept
Point-slope form y - y₁ = m(x - x₁) Through point (x₁,y₁)
Two-point form (y-y₁)/(x-x₁) = (y₂-y₁)/(x₂-x₁) Through two points
General form ax + by + c = 0 Standard JAMB format

1️⃣ Distance Between Points

Distance Formula

Distance formula
d = √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²]
This comes from Pythagoras’ theorem.
Example 1: Basic Distance

Find the distance between A(2,3) and B(5,7).

d = √[(5-2)² + (7-3)²]

= √[3² + 4²] = √[9 + 16] = √25 = 5

Answer: 5 units
Example 2: Find an Unknown Coordinate

The distance between P(3,k) and Q(7,1) is 5 units. Find k.

5 = √[(7-3)² + (1-k)²]

5 = √[16 + (1-k)²]

Square both sides:

25 = 16 + (1-k)²

9 = (1-k)²

1-k = ±3

Case 1: 1-k = 3 → k = -2

Case 2: 1-k = -3 → k = 4

Answer: k = -2 or 4
Distance on the coordinate plane
Distance between two points is the hypotenuse formed by the horizontal and vertical changes.

2️⃣ Midpoint of a Line Segment

Midpoint Formula

Midpoint formula
M = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2)
Average the x-coordinates and average the y-coordinates.
Example 3: Find Midpoint

Find the midpoint of the line joining A(4,6) and B(8,10).

Mₓ = (4+8)/2 = 6

Mᵧ = (6+10)/2 = 8

Answer: M = (6, 8)
Example 4: Find Endpoint from Midpoint

M(5,3) is the midpoint of AB. If A = (2,1), find B.

Let B = (x,y)

5 = (2+x)/2 → 10 = 2+x → x = 8

3 = (1+y)/2 → 6 = 1+y → y = 5

Answer: B = (8, 5)

3️⃣ Gradient (Slope)

Gradient Formula

Gradient formula
m = (y₂-y₁)/(x₂-x₁) = Δy/Δx
  • Positive gradient means the line rises to the right
  • Negative gradient means the line falls to the right
  • Zero gradient means a horizontal line
  • Undefined gradient means a vertical line
Example 5: Basic Gradient

Find the gradient through P(2,5) and Q(6,13).

m = (13-5)/(6-2) = 8/4 = 2

Answer: 2
Example 6: Angle with the x-axis

A line makes an angle of 45° with the positive x-axis. Find its gradient.

m = tan θ = tan 45° = 1

Answer: 1

4️⃣ Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

Line Relationships

Relationship Condition Formula
Parallel Same gradient m₁ = m₂
Perpendicular Negative reciprocals m₁ × m₂ = -1

Example 7: Parallel Lines

Show that the line through A(1,2) and B(4,8) is parallel to the line through C(0,0) and D(2,4).

Gradient of AB:

m₁ = (8-2)/(4-1) = 6/3 = 2

Gradient of CD:

m₂ = (4-0)/(2-0) = 4/2 = 2

Since m₁ = m₂, the lines are parallel.

Answer: The lines are parallel
Example 8: Perpendicular Gradient

Find the gradient of a line perpendicular to a line with gradient 3/4.

m₂ = -1/m₁ = -4/3

Answer: -4/3

5️⃣ Equations of Lines

Four Forms of Line Equations

1. Slope-intercept form
y = mx + c
Use when gradient and y-intercept are known.
2. Point-slope form
y - y₁ = m(x - x₁)
Use when a point and gradient are known.
3. Two-point form
(y-y₁)/(x-x₁) = (y₂-y₁)/(x₂-x₁)
Use when two points are known.
4. General form
ax + by + c = 0
This is the standard JAMB form.
Example 9: Point-Slope Form

Find the equation of the line with gradient 2 passing through (3,5).

y - 5 = 2(x - 3)

y - 5 = 2x - 6

y = 2x - 1

General form: 2x - y - 1 = 0

Answer: y = 2x - 1 or 2x - y - 1 = 0
Example 10: Two-Point Form

Find the equation of the line through A(2,3) and B(4,7).

Step 1: Find the gradient

m = (7-3)/(4-2) = 4/2 = 2

Step 2: Use point-slope form with A(2,3)

y - 3 = 2(x - 2)

y - 3 = 2x - 4

y = 2x - 1

Answer: y = 2x - 1

🔥 JAMB Past Question Style

Type 1: Combined Concepts

The line joining P(3,4) and Q(7,k) has gradient 2. Find k and the distance PQ.

Find k:

2 = (k-4)/(7-3) = (k-4)/4

8 = k - 4

k = 12

Distance PQ:

d = √[(7-3)² + (12-4)²]

= √[16 + 64] = √80 = 4√5

Answer: k = 12, PQ = 4√5
Type 2: Perpendicular Bisector

Find the equation of the perpendicular bisector of AB where A(2,4) and B(6,8).

Step 1: Midpoint

M = ((2+6)/2, (4+8)/2) = (4,6)

Step 2: Gradient of AB

m = (8-4)/(6-2) = 1

Step 3: Gradient of perpendicular line

m⊥ = -1

Step 4: Equation through midpoint

y - 6 = -1(x - 4)

y - 6 = -x + 4

x + y - 10 = 0

Answer: x + y - 10 = 0

💡 Exam Tips

How to score high in coordinate geometry

  • Always sketch a rough diagram
  • Be careful with negative signs
  • Simplify radicals properly, for example √50 = 5√2 and √80 = 4√5
  • When possible, write final line equations in general form
  • Include units for distances if the question gives units
  • Distance: use Pythagoras on coordinate differences
  • Midpoint: average x and average y
  • Gradient: rise over run
  • Parallel: same gradient
  • Perpendicular: product of gradients is -1
  • Point-slope form is often the most flexible line formula
