1️⃣ What is Reaction Rate?

Rate of reaction = change in concentration (or amount) of reactant or product per unit time.

Rate = Δ[product]/Δt

Rate = −Δ[reactant]/Δt

Typical rate curve: steep at first, then flattens as reactants get used up

Graph tip

  • The faster the reaction, the steeper the slope.

2️⃣ Factors That Change the Rate (Collision Theory)

JAMB expects you to name the four major factors and explain each using collision theory.

(i) Temperature

  • Higher T → particles move faster → more frequent collisions
  • More particles have energy ≥ Ea → more successful collisions
  • Rule of thumb: 10°C rise ≈ doubles rate

(ii) Concentration / Pressure

  • Higher concentration → more particles per unit volume → more collisions per second
  • For gases: higher pressure = higher concentration

(iii) Surface Area

  • Smaller particles (powder) → larger surface area → more collisions
  • Powder reacts faster than lumps (same mass)

(iv) Catalyst

  • Provides alternative pathway with lower Ea
  • Not used up during the reaction
Catalyst lowers activation energy (Ea) → more successful collisions

3️⃣ Reaction Rate Curves (What JAMB Wants You to Say)

Typical graphs

  • Reactant concentration vs time: starts steep, then flattens
  • Gas volume vs time: steep at first, then levels off

Effect of factors

  • Higher T / concentration / surface area / catalyst → steeper initial slope
  • Reaction reaches completion sooner
Comparing curves: steeper curve = faster rate

4️⃣ Activation Energy (Ea) & Collision Theory

Activation energy (Ea): the minimum energy particles must have before they can react.

Collision theory (3 conditions)

  • Particles must collide
  • Collision energy must be ≥ Ea
  • Particles must collide with correct orientation
🧠 One sentence

Raising temperature increases both collision frequency and the fraction with energy ≥ Ea.

🧮 6 JAMB Examples (Worked)

Example 1: Average rate from concentration change

[Reactant] falls from 0.8 to 0.2 mol/dm³ in 120 s. Find average rate.

Δ[reactant] = 0.8 − 0.2 = 0.6 mol/dm³

Rate = Δ[reactant]/Δt = 0.6/120 = 0.005 mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹

Example 2: Factor change using time

Time: 40 s at 25°C, 20 s at 35°C. By what factor did rate increase?

Rate ∝ 1/time

Factor = (1/20)/(1/40) = 2

Answer: Rate doubles

Example 3: Surface area comparison

Powdered marble reacts in 10 s; lumps take 80 s. Factor faster?

Factor = (1/10)/(1/80) = 8

Answer: Powder is 8× faster

Example 4: Catalyst factor

H₂O₂ decomposes in 120 s without MnO₂; 15 s with MnO₂. Factor?

Factor = 120/15 = 8

Answer: Rate increases 8×

Example 5: Initial rate from graph

Tangent at t = 0 has slope 0.04 cm³/s. Find initial rate.

Initial rate = slope of tangent at t=0 = 0.04 cm³ s⁻¹

Example 6: Explain 10°C rule using collision theory

Higher T → faster particles → more collisions

More particles have energy ≥ Ea (big increase)

More successful collisions → rate roughly doubles
