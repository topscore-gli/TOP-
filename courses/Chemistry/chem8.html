1️⃣ Definition of Acids

An acid is a substance whose aqueous solution furnishes H₃O⁺ (hydronium) ions, OR a proton (H⁺) donor.

Acids produce hydronium ions (H₃O⁺) in water

Properties of acids (7 points)

  • Sour taste (e.g., lemon)
  • Turn blue litmus → red
  • Corrosive (burn skin)
  • Conduct electricity (ions present)
  • React with metals → H₂ gas
  • React with bases → salt + water (neutralisation)
  • React with CO₃²⁻/HCO₃⁻ salts → CO₂

Examples

Type Examples
Inorganic acids HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃
Organic acids CH₃COOH (vinegar), citric acid (oranges), tartaric acid (grapes)

2️⃣ Basicity of an Acid

Basicity = number of replaceable H⁺ ions per molecule of an acid.

Type Examples Basicity
Monobasic HCl, HNO₃, CH₃COOH 1
Dibasic H₂SO₄, H₂CO₃ 2
Tribasic H₃PO₄ 3

⚠️ Quick check

  • Count the “replaceable H” in the formula (for common acids in JAMB).

3️⃣ Definition of Bases

A base is a substance that furnishes OH⁻ ions in water, OR a proton acceptor.

Bases produce hydroxide ions (OH⁻) in water

Properties of bases

  • Bitter taste
  • Soapy/slippery feel
  • Turn red litmus → blue
  • Corrosive (strong ones like NaOH)
  • Conduct electricity (ions present)

Alkalis = soluble bases (e.g., NaOH, KOH, NH₃(aq), Ca(OH)₂).

5️⃣ Strong vs Weak Acids/Bases (Ionisation + Conductance)

Strength (not concentration)

  • Strong acid: completely ionises (HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃) → very low pH, high conductance
  • Weak acid: partially ionises (CH₃COOH) → higher pH (same conc.), low conductance
  • Strong base: completely ionises (NaOH, KOH) → high pH, high conductance
  • Weak base: partially ionises (NH₃(aq)) → lower pH (same conc.), low conductance

Qualitative conductance

  • At same concentration: strong acid/base >> weak acid/base (because more ions).

7️⃣ Methods of Preparing Salts

  • Neutralisation: acid + base → salt + water
  • Acid + metal → salt + H₂
  • Acid + trioxocarbonate(IV) → salt + CO₂ + H₂O
  • Acid + basic oxide → salt + water
  • Precipitation (double decomposition): two soluble salts → insoluble salt (ppt) + soluble salt
Neutralisation: acid + base → salt + water

8️⃣ pH and pOH Scale

pH = −log₁₀[H₃O⁺]

pOH = −log₁₀[OH⁻]

pH + pOH = 14 (at 25°C)

pH scale: <7 acidic, 7 neutral, >7 basic

9️⃣ Acid/Base Titrations (Which Indicator?)

Titration type Suitable indicator
Strong acid + strong base Phenolphthalein or methyl orange

Why indicators differ

  • End-point pH changes depending on strength of acid/base.

🔟 Hydrolysis of Salts (pH Prediction Rules)

Rules (memorise)

  • Strong acid + strong base → neutral (pH = 7)
  • Strong acid + weak base → acidic
  • Weak acid + strong base → basic

Example: NH₄Cl is from HCl (strong) + NH₃ (weak) → solution is acidic.

🧮 6 JAMB Calculation Examples (Worked)

Example 1: pH of strong acid

Find pH of 0.01 M HCl

Strong acid → [H⁺] = 0.01 = 10⁻²

pH = −log(10⁻²) = 2

Example 2: pH of strong base

Find pH of 0.001 M NaOH

[OH⁻] = 0.001 = 10⁻³

pOH = 3

pH = 14 − 3 = 11

Example 3: pH of weak acid

0.1 M CH₃COOH, Kₐ = 1.8×10⁻⁵

[H⁺] = √(Kₐ×C) = √(1.8×10⁻⁵ × 0.1) = √(1.8×10⁻⁶) ≈ 1.34×10⁻³

pH = −log(1.34×10⁻³) ≈ 2.87

Example 4: Titration (find concentration, 1:1)

25 cm³ 0.1 M HCl neutralises 20 cm³ NaOH. Find [NaOH].

Moles HCl = 0.1 × 25/1000 = 0.0025 mol

Moles NaOH = 0.0025 mol

[NaOH] = 0.0025 × 1000/20 = 0.125 M

Example 5: Titration (different n)

Volume of 0.2 M NaOH to neutralise 30 cm³ 0.15 M H₂SO₄

Moles H₂SO₄ = 0.15 × 30/1000 = 0.0045 mol

H₂SO₄ is dibasic → needs 2 moles NaOH per 1 mole acid

Moles NaOH = 2 × 0.0045 = 0.009 mol

V(NaOH) = moles/M = 0.009/0.2 = 0.045 L = 45 cm³

Example 6: Hydrolysis pH prediction

Nature of 0.1 M NH₄Cl solution

NH₄Cl = strong acid (HCl) + weak base (NH₃)

NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺

Produces H₃O⁺ → acidic (pH < 7)
