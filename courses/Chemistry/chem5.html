1️⃣ Chemical Reaction vs Nuclear Reaction

Key point: In chemical reactions the nucleus stays the same. In nuclear reactions the nucleus itself changes.

Chemical reactions rearrange electrons; nuclear reactions change the nucleus
Feature Ordinary chemical reaction Nuclear reaction
What changes Electrons (outer shells) Protons + neutrons (nucleus)
Element changes? No (same element) Yes (transmutation)
Mass change Very tiny (not measurable) Large (measurable)
Energy Small Extremely large
Example 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O ²³⁵₉₂U + ¹₀n → fission products + energy

2️⃣ Radioactivity (α, β, γ) + Detection

Radioactivity: unstable nuclei emit radiation spontaneously to become stable.

Penetration: α stopped by paper, β by aluminium, γ needs lead/concrete

Properties of α, β, γ (very important table)

Property Alpha (α) Beta (β⁻) Gamma (γ)
Nature Helium nucleus (⁴₂He) Electron (⁰₋₁e) Electromagnetic wave
Charge +2 −1 0
Mass 4 u ~0 0
Speed ~10% of light ~90% of light Speed of light
Penetration Very low (paper/skin) Medium (1 mm Al) Very high (lead/concrete)
Ionizing power Very high Medium Very low
Deflection in field Deflected (+) Deflected (opposite α) Not deflected

Detection of radiation (know at least 3)

  • Geiger–Muller (G-M) counter: most tested (clicks/count rate)
  • Photographic film (blackens)
  • Cloud chamber (visible tracks)
  • Scintillation counter

3️⃣ Natural vs Artificial Radioactivity

Natural: spontaneous decay in nature (e.g., U-238 in rocks decays to Pb).

Artificial (induced): made by bombarding a stable nucleus with particles (reactor/cyclotron).

Natural radioactivity often happens as decay series (chains)

Example (induced): ¹⁴₇N + ⁴₂He → ¹⁷₈O + ¹₁H

🧠 Easy memory

Natural = spontaneous in nature • Artificial = induced by man (bombardment).

4️⃣ Nuclear Stability & Radioactive Decay

Stability depends largely on neutron/proton ratio (n/p). If unstable, the nucleus decays.

Common decay equations

  • Alpha decay (heavy nuclei): ²²⁶₈₈Ra → ²²²₈₆Rn + ⁴₂He
  • Beta-minus (too many neutrons): ¹⁴₆C → ¹⁴₇N + ⁰₋₁e
  • Gamma emission: no change in A or Z (only energy released)

Quick changes (memorize)

  • α emission: A decreases by 4, Z decreases by 2
  • β⁻ emission: A unchanged, Z increases by 1
  • γ emission: A and Z unchanged

5️⃣ Fission and Fusion

Fission splits heavy nuclei; fusion joins light nuclei

Nuclear fission (splitting)

Heavy nucleus splits into smaller nuclei, releasing energy and more neutrons (can cause chain reaction).

Example: ²³⁵₉₂U + ¹₀n → fission products + 3¹₀n + energy

Use: nuclear power stations

Nuclear fusion (joining)

Light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing a lot of energy (requires very high temperature).

Example: ²₁H + ³₁H → ⁴₂He + ¹₀n + energy

Where: the sun/stars

Feature Fission Fusion
What happens Splits heavy nucleus Joins light nuclei
Conditions Neutron bombardment (reactor) Very high temperature/pressure
Where seen Nuclear power plants Sun and stars
Waste Radioactive waste produced Less long-lived waste (in theory)

6️⃣ Half-Life (T½) Calculations

Half-life (T½): time taken for half of the radioactive atoms in a sample to decay.

Radioactive decay curve (exponential): decreases by half each half-life

Formula: Fraction remaining = (1/2)n

where n = time / T½

Fast examples

  • T½ = 4 days, after 12 days → n = 3 → fraction left = 1/8
  • 16 g (T½ = 8 days), after 24 days → n = 3 → mass left = 2 g

7️⃣ Balancing Simple Nuclear Equations

Rule: total mass number (A) and atomic number (Z) must balance on both sides.

Examples

  • ²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Th + ⁴₂He
  • ¹⁴₆C → ¹⁴₇N + ⁰₋₁e

How to complete a missing product (fast)

  • Missing A = (A on left) − (A of known particles)
  • Missing Z = (Z on left) − (Z of known particles)

📝 3 JAMB Examples (Solved)

Example 1: Half-life fraction

Question: T½ = 5 days. Fraction remaining after 20 days?

n = 20/5 = 4

Fraction left = (1/2)⁴ = 1/16

Example 2: Complete the equation

Question: ²²⁶₈₈Ra → ? + ⁴₂He

Mass: 226 = A + 4 → A = 222

Atomic: 88 = Z + 2 → Z = 86

So product is ²²²₈₆Rn

Example 3: Application

Question: Which radioisotope is used for cancer treatment?

Answer: Cobalt-60 (γ-rays)
